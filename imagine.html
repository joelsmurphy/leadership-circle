04 Movement 04 — Futures of Innovation

Imagine

Envisioning faithful, love-motivated innovation toward 2040

Orientation

Imagination is not optimism. It is the capacity to hold a genuine alternative in view long enough for it to become thinkable — and then, slowly, possible. Most leaders are operating with a narrower picture of the future than they realize, shaped by what has failed before, who has permission to speak, and what exhaustion has quietly ruled out. This movement does not ask you to manufacture hope. It asks you to notice where your imagination has been contracting — and to practice opening it, deliberately, toward what love might require.

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

I've lost confidence that significant change is possible in my context. +
That loss is real and deserves to be named, not performed over. What specifically has eroded your confidence? Is it a particular failure, a pattern of disappointment, or the weight of accumulated small losses?
I can imagine change for others, but not for my own community. +
That asymmetry is worth paying attention to. What is different about your context that makes imagination harder — and are those differences real, or are they partly the story your fatigue is telling?
I have a clear picture of what I hope for, but it feels impossible from where I'm standing. +
Hold that picture. The gap between where you are and where you hope to be is not necessarily evidence that the hope is wrong. What is one thing that could be different without requiring everything to change at once?
I'm genuinely uncertain what faithfulness looks like for my community in the next decade. +
That uncertainty is appropriate — and it can be generative rather than paralyzing. This movement invites you to imagine not from certainty but from love. What does loving your community well require you to be open to?
I feel imaginative and energized, but my imagination isn't shared by the people I lead. +
That gap matters. Unarticulated imagination stays private and cannot do communal work. What would it take to make your imagination visible and accessible to the people you lead?
"The task before Christian leaders is not to secure the future, but to respond faithfully within it. Sanctified imagination is not optimism — it is the practice of holding possible futures in view long enough for discernment to do its work."
Interpretive Field Guide — Futuring Hub
Scenario — 2040

A ministry community in Calgary — started in 2029 by six people who met during a formation circle — now serves two hundred families across three neighbourhoods. They have no building of their own. They run no programs that require significant ongoing budget. They gather twice a month for shared prayer and discernment, and weekly in smaller groups organized around neighbourhood and vocation.

Three of the six founding members could not have described this outcome in 2026. They were just trying to be honest about what they could actually sustain.

This is not a prediction. It is a provocation. What would have to be true about your community's formation between now and 2040 for something like this to be possible?

Reflect
  1. If the question driving innovation in your context were "how can we love people well given this new context?" rather than "how can we stay relevant?" — what would change? What would you stop doing, and what would you start?
  2. The field guide asks: what lies beyond the boundaries of your current innovation imagination? What possibilities feel genuinely out of reach — and what is keeping them there? Is it capacity, courage, permission, or something else?
  3. Whose imagination is missing from your community's vision for the future? Whose experience of 2040 are you not yet imagining with?
Challenge

Whose Future Are You Imagining?

The limits of our imagination are often the limits of whose experience we've built into our thinking.

"When you picture the future of your ministry community, whose experience is at the center? Whose is at the edges — or missing entirely? What would change if you began from the perspective of the person least well-served by your current imagination of the future?"

Group Practice

Preferred Futures Conversation

  1. Invite your team or leadership group to spend ten minutes individually answering: "It is 2035. Our ministry community has become known for one thing. What is it?"
  2. Share responses aloud. Record every image and phrase — do not evaluate yet.
  3. Ask together: What does love appear to be demanding in these responses? What fears are quietly shaping the pictures?
  4. Identify the one image or phrase your group is least comfortable with — and ask: what is that discomfort revealing?
  5. Close with this question: "What is one thing we could do differently in the next ninety days that points, however imperfectly, toward what we imagined?"
Micro-Practice

One Paragraph from 2040

Set a timer for twelve minutes. Write one paragraph set in 2040, describing your ministry community as you most hope it will be. Write in the present tense. Do not stop to edit. Begin: "It is 2040. In our community..."

Why it matters

The act of writing a possible future — even briefly, even imperfectly — activates a different kind of thinking than analysis does. You are not forecasting. You are practicing the posture of sanctified imagination.

Group version

Each person writes independently. Read them aloud without attribution and notice what images, values, and hopes appear across the group. Where do they converge? Where do they reveal real differences in vision that haven't yet been named?

Future Snapshot

A Ministry Without a Building — 2031

A team of eight — a pastor, a nurse practitioner, two seminary students, a retired teacher, and three people in recovery — has been meeting in a community centre on the north side of a mid-size Canadian city for four years. They don't call it a church. They call it a table. They gather weekly for a meal and thirty minutes of what they call "honest attention" — structured listening to what's hard in each other's lives and in the neighbourhood.

Three of the eight did not grow up Christian. The pastor has not preached a traditional sermon in two years. The budget is twelve thousand dollars. The waiting list to join is eighteen people long.

This is not a template. It is a provocation. What does it open in you?

What has imagining 2040 opened in you that analysis of the present could not reach? Where did hope surface? What fears did the exercise reveal — and what might those fears be protecting? Has Dean's reframing — from innovation to love — changed anything in how you're thinking?
