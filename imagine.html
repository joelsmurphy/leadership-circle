01 Encounter

A snapshot from 2040.
Read slowly. Notice what it provokes.

This is not a prediction. It is a provocation — a deliberately specific picture of one possible future, offered to stretch the range of what you can consider. Notice what you feel as you read it.

Horizon Snapshot — 2040

The three remaining churches in the county share a single ordained minister, three part-time lay leaders, and a building that no one owns outright. Sunday gatherings rotate between houses. The music is downloaded and the sermon is sometimes prerecorded. Half the congregation has never attended in person. The other half hasn't been inside a church building since 2029.

And yet: there is a food sovereignty network that grew out of a Wednesday morning study. There is a palliative care accompaniment team that trained thirty-seven volunteers in four years. There is a theology podcast with eleven thousand regular listeners who have never heard of the county. The minister says she has never felt more like a minister in her life.

The future of faithful ministry will not look like the past. The question is whether you can imagine it — and whether what you're imagining is limited by what you've already lost.

03 Reframe

Three stances toward an uncertain future.

The field guide identifies three orientations that shape how communities approach innovation. These are not strategies — they are deep motivational frameworks. Choose the one that most honestly describes how your community currently orients toward its future.

Love

We innovate because we are called to love people we are not yet reaching — not because we are afraid of what happens if we don't. The driving question is: Who is left out, and what does love require of us?

What would we be willing to change if love — not survival — were the real driver?
Relevance

We innovate to remain meaningful in a changing cultural landscape — to prove that faith still speaks to real life. The driving question is: What do we need to become in order to be taken seriously?

What are we performing for an imagined audience — and is that performance forming us faithfully?
Survival

We innovate because we fear what happens if we don't. The driving question is: How do we keep the institution going? This is often unnamed — presented as vision when it is actually fear.

What would we do differently if survival were off the table as a motivation?

04 Apply

Write the future you can almost see.

This is not a strategic plan. It is an act of imagination — practiced, disciplined, and honest. Use the scaffold below. The seed text in each section is not a constraint; it is a starting point. Push past it.

Imaginative Exercise — 2040 It is 2040. The ministry community I lead or belong to has changed in ways that surprised us...

What does it look like? Be specific — not "we are thriving" but what that actually means in practice, location, relationship, form.

Who is present who was not present before? Whose absence has been part of the cost?

What did we have to release to get here? What did we hold on to — and was that the right call?

What has remained constant — what are the things that, despite everything, have held?

Challenge

The Imagination You've Settled For

Most ministry communities aren't suffering from a lack of vision statements. They're suffering from a failure of actual imagination — the capacity to picture something genuinely different from what already exists. Vision statements often project a better version of the present, not a genuinely new future.

"What you just wrote — how different is it, really, from what you already have? What would it take to write something that actually surprised you? Try again. Push further."

What is the emerging possibility?

Emerging Possibility — Futures of Innovation
The image I'm carrying forward

Not a plan — a picture. What is the most compelling thing you've imagined in this movement?

What changed in my imagination

What can you now picture that you couldn't before — or what can you no longer picture that once seemed certain?

What remains essential

Across every version of the future you've considered — what cannot be surrendered? What must be present in any faithful version of what comes next?

For teams and leadership groups — Shared Imagination Exercise +
  1. Ask each person to complete the 2040 writing exercise individually — before the group gathers.
  2. In the group, invite each person to share only what surprised them about their own writing. Not what they wrote — what surprised them.
  3. Ask together: Where do our imaginations overlap — and where are they genuinely different? What does the difference reveal?
  4. Name together what we are most afraid to imagine — and ask why that fear is so strong.
  5. Close by identifying one shared image that the group can carry forward into readiness work. It doesn't have to be agreed on — just named.
