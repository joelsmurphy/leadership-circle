04 Movement 04 — Futures of Innovation

Imagine

Envisioning faithful, love-motivated innovation toward 2040

Orientation

The field guide opens with a provocation: Imagine Canada in 2040. The pace of change has not slowed. Economic pressure, AI embedded across every domain, persistent polarization, shifting demographics, ecological disruption — and within all of this, Christian leaders continuing to ask: how do we respond faithfully?

This is not a forecast. It is an invitation to sanctified imagination — the prophetic capacity to hold a possible future in view and ask what faithfulness requires within it. The field guide understands innovation not as strategy or novelty, but as an act of hopeful imagination grounded in discernment and made possible through collaboration.

Kendra Creasy Dean names what is at stake: "The truth is that innovation is the wrong word for our impulse to find new forms of ministry and new ways of being the church. The word we are groping for is love." This movement asks: what would it look like to organize your imagination around love rather than around innovation? And what does that reorientation require of how you lead?

Reflect
  1. If the question driving innovation in your context were "how can we love people well given this new context?" rather than "how can we stay relevant?" — what would change? What would you stop doing, and what would you start?
  2. The field guide asks: what lies beyond the boundaries of your current innovation imagination? What possibilities feel genuinely out of reach — and what is keeping them there? Is it capacity, courage, permission, or something else?
  3. Whose imagination is missing from your community's vision for the future? Whose experience of 2040 are you not yet imagining with?
Go Deeper

"The task before Christian leaders is not to secure the future, but to respond faithfully within it." What would it feel like to genuinely release the need to secure the future — and what kind of imagination would that release?

In Conversation

Invite your team or a trusted group into a preferred futures conversation grounded in the 2040 frame:

"Imagine your ministry community in 2040 — not the one we're trying to preserve, but the one we're hoping to become. What is it doing? What has it stopped doing? Who is it serving, and how? If faithfulness were the measure rather than growth or relevance, what would be different?"

Don't evaluate yet. Let the imagination of the group be larger than any individual's vision. Collect what emerges.

Practice

Write a one-paragraph future story set in 2040. Describe your ministry community as you most hope it will be — not as you fear it will be, and not as you think it is likely to be, but as you genuinely hope it could be. Write in the present tense, as if it has already happened.

Begin: "It is 2040. In our community..."

Read it back. Then ask: what in this story is shaped by love? And what concrete step, however small, could move us toward even one part of it?

Notice

After sitting with this movement, reflect:

What has imagining 2040 opened in you that analysis of the present could not reach? Where did hope surface? What fears did the exercise reveal — and what might those fears be protecting? Has Dean's reframing — from innovation to love — changed anything in how you're thinking?
