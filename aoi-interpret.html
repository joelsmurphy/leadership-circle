01 Encounter

Two churches. Same instability.
Completely different interpretations.

Read both slowly. Notice what each is saying about God, the future, and the community's role. Notice where you feel resistance — and where you feel recognition.

Community A — Fortress Interpretation

The congregation has watched its neighbourhood change dramatically over fifteen years. They have responded by strengthening what they already have: the youth program is more robust, the Sunday service more carefully curated, the building more maintained. The pastor talks about holding the line — about being a stable presence in an unstable world. "People need something that doesn't change," he says, "and we can be that."

The congregation feels secure. But newcomers to the neighbourhood — many of whom are younger, more racially diverse, economically precarious — rarely come back after a first visit. The things the community has held stable are not the things that feel relevant or welcoming to the people who have moved in. The pastor is not wrong that people need stability. But the community he is stabilizing does not include them.

Community B — Compass Interpretation

The congregation is smaller and poorer than it was five years ago. Three long-standing families have left. The building fund is gone. The pastor has stopped pretending this is a temporary disruption. At a recent board meeting, she said something that surprised everyone: "I think instability is our formation context now — not an interruption to it. We have to become the kind of community that can hold uncertainty without collapsing."

The congregation has started doing things it would not have considered before: sharing pastoral care with another denomination, hosting a climate anxiety group for people who are not members, partnering with a local food network it used to only donate to. The pastor doesn't know where this leads. She knows what questions are orienting the journey — and she has stopped expecting those questions to resolve quickly.

03 Reframe

Fortress or Compass?

The Age of Instability report names a central interpretive fork: communities that interpret their calling as maintaining a stable fortress against change, and communities that interpret instability as a compass-orienting journey. These are not strategies — they are theological orientations. Neither is entirely wrong. Read what each actually claims.

Fortress Orientation Stability as Faithfulness

The calling of the community is to provide a stable, unchanging witness in an unstable world. What the community holds constant — its liturgy, its doctrine, its culture, its building — is itself a form of prophetic resistance to disorder. Change is managed carefully, if at all. The primary virtue is continuity.

The risk: A community that cannot move with what God is doing in the world cannot be a compass for others navigating it. Stability offered to a changing world can become inaccessibility without anyone noticing the difference.

Compass Orientation Presence as Faithfulness

The calling of the community is to remain oriented to God's direction even as everything else shifts — to be a compass that helps others navigate, not a fortress that holds them apart from what's happening. This requires the community itself to be formed by instability, not merely to survive it. The primary virtue is adaptive fidelity.

The risk: A community that prizes adaptability without a stable centre can lose its theological integrity. Not every direction the community might move is the right one. Compass without True North is just drift.

04 Apply

Interpret what is actually happening in your context.

Three prompts, structured to move from observation to theological meaning to community accountability. Write honestly — the goal is interpretation, not resolution.

A
What is instability actually doing to your community?

Go beneath the presenting challenges. What is instability revealing, exposing, or threatening about what your community is and how it understands itself?

B
What theological conviction most needs to be heard right now?

Not the conviction that is most comforting or most familiar — but the one that is most necessary for your community to hear and believe in this specific season.

C
What story is your community telling itself about instability?

Every community has a narrative about what is happening and why. What story is yours telling — and is it faithful to what is actually there, or is it protecting something that needs to be questioned?

Challenge

The Interpretation That Protects You

Every interpretive framework protects something — a sense of identity, a way of doing things, a community's sense of its own continuity. The frameworks we are least willing to question are often the ones protecting the things we are most afraid to lose.

"Name the interpretation of instability in your community that, if it were wrong, would require you to change something significant about how you lead, how you allocate resources, or how you understand your community's calling. Why is that interpretation so difficult to question?"

Micro-Practice — The Reframe Question

This week, when you encounter a community challenge — a budget constraint, a program difficulty, a relational conflict — pause before you interpret it as a management problem. Ask instead: What is this revealing about who we are and what we actually believe? Then write down what you see.

Why it matters

Leaders trained in management tend to interpret instability through the lens of problem-solving. Theological interpretation requires a different posture — one that asks what God is doing in the disruption, not just how to stabilize it.

Group version

At your next team gathering, bring a current challenge and ask the group to offer three different theological interpretations of what it means — not solutions, but readings. Notice which interpretations generate energy, which generate fear, and which the group wants to avoid.

Tension

The Theology That Instability Requires

Theology developed in seasons of stability tends to counsel endurance and faithfulness. But stability-formed theology can be inadequate to a season of genuine disruption — not because it is wrong, but because it was formed in conditions that no longer obtain. The church has always had to re-interpret its inheritance in light of new contexts.

The question is not whether to do theology in instability, but whether to do it with enough honesty to let the instability actually challenge what we believe — not just confirm it. This is harder than it sounds. It requires the willingness to say: what I inherited may not be enough for where we are going.

For teams and leadership groups — Interpretation in Community +
  1. Read the two encounter scenarios (Fortress and Compass) as a group. Ask each person: which community do we more closely resemble — and what do we think about that?
  2. Invite each person to name one word that describes the emotional register they bring to instability in your context. Write them all on a whiteboard or shared document. Notice the range.
  3. Ask together: What theological conviction is our community most relying on to make sense of what is happening? Is that conviction adequate to what we are actually experiencing?
  4. Invite someone to name one perspective on instability that your leadership team does not have access to — and make a plan to seek it out.
  5. Close by naming together: Are we operating from a fortress orientation, a compass orientation, or something more complicated? What would it mean to move deliberately toward one of them?
