Self-Curated Spirituality — Futuring Hub

Next Steps

Five practice pathways for faithful engagement in a self-curating spiritual culture

These pathways are drawn from the leadership implications in the Futuring Hub's Self-Curated Spirituality report. They are not programs to implement or frameworks to download. They are invitations — starting points for communities that have done the sensemaking work of this circle and are ready to ask what faithful response actually looks like from where they stand.

Each pathway names a practice, the theological conviction underneath it, and a first concrete step. Begin with whichever most honestly connects to where your community is right now.

01 Pathway

Reimagine Formation for a Hybrid Spiritual Future

Stop designing formation around the assumption that people will show up weekly, progress linearly, and commit in advance. Start designing formation that meets people in the actual rhythms and conditions of a hybrid, self-curating spiritual life.

The Practice

Audit your community's current formation offerings through one question: Are these designed for people who are already inside and committed — or do they create genuine on-ramps for people who are spiritually curious, mid-deconstruction, or building faith from scattered starting points?

Then identify one formation practice or space your community could offer that is genuinely accessible to people building their faith outside traditional structures — not a seeker-sensitive program, but something with real theological depth that doesn't require prior commitment as the condition of entry. Start small. Design for relationship, not for scale.

Theological Conviction

Formation Precedes Commitment, Not the Other Way Around

The New Testament pattern of formation is apprenticeship before confession, not confession before apprenticeship. People were drawn into the community's life and gradually formed by it — not required to produce theological clarity as the condition of participation. Self-curated spirituality is, in part, a response to communities that inverted this pattern. Reimagining formation means recovering it.

First Step

Identify one person in your network who is genuinely spiritually curious but has no current connection to any faith community. Ask if you can have coffee with them and hear about their spiritual life. Ask what they are looking for, what they have found, and what they wish existed but doesn't. Then bring what you heard to your leadership team — not as a data point, but as a person. Let their experience shape one concrete change in how your community approaches formation.

02 Pathway

Equip Leaders for Decentralized and Relational Ministry

Self-curated spirituality is reshaping not just how people form faith but how they relate to authority, expertise, and institutional leadership. Faithful response requires leaders who can function in flatter, more participatory, and more relational ministry structures.

The Practice

Invest deliberately in the formation of leaders who can hold theological depth without needing to hold positional authority — leaders who form faith through relationship rather than through programs, who can accompany people mid-deconstruction without needing to resolve their questions, and who can hold the tradition's gifts with open hands rather than clenched fists.

This is a formation investment, not a competency training. It requires honest examination of how your current leadership formation produces the kind of leaders — and the kind of authority — your community is actually embodying. If your leadership formation produces people who are theologically confident but relationally inflexible, it is producing the wrong leaders for this moment.

Theological Conviction

Authority Earned Through Presence, Not Claimed Through Position

In a self-curating culture, positional authority carries less weight than relational credibility. People trust leaders who know them, have walked with them through difficulty, and have demonstrated that their theological convictions are lived rather than merely stated. The tradition has always known this — the rabbinical model, the monastic model, the catechumenate — were all built on apprenticeship and relationship, not on positional authority alone. Recovering these patterns is not accommodation to culture. It is recovery of the tradition's own wisdom.

First Step

Name one leader in your community — formal or informal — who currently embodies the kind of relational, theologically grounded, non-anxious presence that a self-curating culture can actually receive. Then make a plan to learn from them, not about their techniques but about their formation: What shaped them? What do they believe about their role? What have they had to let go of? Let their embodied wisdom inform how you think about developing other leaders.

03 Pathway

Shift from Attractional to Embodied and Relational Models

The attractional church model — designed to draw people in through compelling programming, polished worship, and clear communication — is increasingly insufficient for a culture in which people construct their own spiritual experiences from wherever they find them. Embodied, relational, and incarnational presence is not a strategy for post-attractional ministry. It is a recovery of the church's most ancient form.

The Practice

Conduct a simple audit: over the next month, where does your community show up in your neighbourhood or city in ways that have nothing to do with getting people to come to your building or programs? Where are your members genuinely present — relationally, not strategically — in the lives of people who are building their faith outside any church community?

The goal of the audit is not to evaluate effectiveness. It is to make visible what is already present and what is absent. Communities that are genuinely embedded in their neighbourhoods — through relationships, through common life, through shared work and grief — are far more visible to self-curating spiritual seekers than communities whose entire presence is organized around Sunday morning attendance.

Theological Conviction

Incarnation Is the Pattern, Not a Strategy

The incarnation is not a missional technique — it is the shape of God's engagement with humanity. God does not draw people toward a location. God enters the location where people already are. Embodied, relational, place-based ministry is not a post-attractional innovation. It is the church's oldest and most theologically coherent form of witness. The question is whether communities that have organized themselves around attractional models can recover it without simply adding it as another program.

First Step

Identify one neighbourhood, network, or community of people in your context where self-curated spirituality is visible and vibrant — where people are genuinely seeking and forming faith outside any church connection. Then name one way your community could be genuinely present in that space without any agenda to bring people to your building. Not a program. A presence. What would it take to show up there?

04 Pathway

Engage Theologically with Digital and AI-Shaped Spirituality

Digital platforms and algorithmic content are now among the primary environments in which people form their spiritual lives. Faithful engagement with self-curated spirituality requires communities that understand these environments well enough to engage them with theological wisdom — neither avoiding them nor adopting them uncritically.

The Practice

Develop theological literacy in your leadership team around digital and AI-shaped formation: How are algorithms shaping what spiritual content people encounter? What are the theological characteristics of the spiritual content that performs well on digital platforms — and what does the algorithm systematically under-surface? What can AI-mediated spiritual formation not offer — and how does your community offer those things?

This is not a call to produce better digital content, though that may be part of it. It is a call to understand the formation ecology your people are actually inhabiting — and to engage it with the same theological seriousness the church has always brought to the formation environments of every era.

Theological Conviction

Every Medium Shapes What It Carries

The church has always had to think theologically about the formation environments it inhabits — from oral culture to manuscript to print to broadcast to digital. The questions are not new. What is new is the algorithmic curation of spiritual content: platforms that surface what generates engagement rather than what produces formation, optimized for attention rather than for depth. Theological engagement with AI-shaped spirituality means asking not just what content appears but what kind of formation it is capable of producing — and what it structurally cannot produce.

First Step

Spend thirty minutes this week exploring the digital spiritual landscape as a curious observer rather than a critic: follow one or two spiritual accounts or podcasts your community members actually engage with. Notice what they offer, what questions they address, what kind of formation they are capable of producing — and what they cannot offer. Bring what you notice to a leadership conversation and ask together: What gap does our community fill that these don't? Are we actually filling it?

05 Pathway

Offer the Depth That Self-Curation Cannot Produce

Self-curated spirituality has real limits — not because the longing is wrong, but because some things can only be formed in sustained community, over time, through a tradition rich enough to hold both certainty and doubt. Offer that. Not defensively, not as a critique of self-curation, but as a genuine gift.

The Practice

Invest in the depth of your community's formation culture — not to make it more impressive, but to make it more genuinely nourishing to people who are hungry for what algorithms and self-curation cannot give them: sustained accountability, serious theological engagement, honest space for doubt and deconstruction within a community that holds something together, and the formation that comes only through shared life over time.

This means taking your own tradition's formation practices seriously — not as programs but as gifts: contemplative prayer, serious theological study, communal discernment, confession and forgiveness, lament and celebration, sabbath and feast. A community that practices these things seriously and offers them generously will be visible to people who are searching for depth in a landscape of spiritual snacking.

Theological Conviction

The Tradition's Gifts Are Not Yet Exhausted

Self-curated spirituality sometimes signals not that the tradition has failed but that it has been offered poorly — reduced to performance, stripped of contemplative depth, organized around attendance rather than formation, and made to feel culturally narrow when its resources are actually culturally inexhaustible. The tradition has resources for this moment: centuries of wisdom about how to hold doubt and faith together, how to form character through community, how to orient a life toward something larger than personal preference. These are not obsolete gifts. They are often exactly what people searching through algorithmically curated content are unable to find.

First Step

Name one formation practice from your tradition that your community currently offers — or could offer — that self-curated spirituality structurally cannot provide. Not a program or an event, but a practice: regular communal lament, sustained theological study, contemplative prayer, honest accountability, multi-generational shared life, confession and reconciliation. Then ask: Are we currently offering this practice in a way that is accessible to someone who is building faith from outside our tradition? If not, what would it take to offer it differently?
