01 Movement 01 — Futures of Innovation

Attend

Paying attention to the forces already shaping innovation around you

Orientation

The Futuring Hub's Interpretive Field Guide begins with a foundational claim: before we can discern what faithful innovation requires, we must first slow down to notice what is already shaping it. Five compound drivers are identified — not as problems to solve, but as forces to attend to.

These drivers include the rise of network-oriented, ecosystem approaches to innovation; diminished imagination among leaders operating under chronic fatigue and vocational strain; the growing influence of AI and digital technologies on how innovation is imagined and practiced; the precarious ambivalence of holding competing values under sustained overload; and the constraining instability of economic, demographic, and institutional pressures that reduce margin and increase risk.

Attending to these drivers is a theological act. What we choose to notice reveals what we believe matters. This movement invites you to turn toward what is actually shaping innovation in your context — including what you may have been too busy, or too afraid, to look at directly.

Scenario

It is 2027. A small church in rural Nova Scotia has lost its third pastor in four years. Its council chair reads about a network of three Halifax churches that recently launched a shared ministry innovation team. She asks: "Why does innovation always seem to happen somewhere else?" Her question surfaces without self-pity — it is a genuine inquiry into the conditions that make innovation possible or impossible in a given context.

The question beneath her question is the one this movement invites you to sit with: What forces are shaping innovation in your context right now — and are you genuinely attending to them?

Reflect
  1. Which of the five compound drivers resonates most strongly in your context right now — ecosystem shifts, diminished imagination, AI and digital change, ambivalence, or constraining instability? What makes it feel most present?
  2. What signals of change related to innovation are you noticing in your ministry context that you haven't yet named out loud? What are you observing at the edges?
  3. Where have you been too busy, too overwhelmed, or too uncertain to attend carefully to what is shaping innovation in your organization? What has that cost you?
Go Deeper

What signals about the future of innovation might you be dismissing because they are inconvenient, unfamiliar, or too costly to take seriously?

In Conversation

Invite a colleague or a small circle of peers into this question:

"Which of the five compound drivers do you find hardest to attend to in your context — and why do you think that is? What would it cost us to take it seriously?"

Listen more than you speak. Notice what others are seeing that you are not. Resist the urge to move quickly from noticing to problem-solving.

Practice

This week, keep a signal log specific to innovation. At the end of each day, write down two or three observations — moments, conversations, or decisions — where you noticed one of the five compound drivers at work in your context.

Don't interpret yet. Just notice and name. What is already taking shape around you that you haven't yet brought into the open?

Notice

After practicing attention for a few days, sit with these questions:

What are you noticing about what you notice? Have any of the five drivers surprised you by how present they are? What has been harder to attend to than you expected — and what does that resistance tell you about your organization's relationship with innovation?
