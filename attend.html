01 Movement 01 — Futures of Innovation

Attend

Paying attention to the forces already shaping innovation around you

Orientation

Most of us move too quickly from noticing to explaining. We see a signal — declining engagement, a restless conversation, a budget that's tighter than it should be — and we immediately reach for a story about what it means. This movement slows that reflex. Before you interpret or decide, you are invited simply to look. What is already present? What are you noticing at the edges? And what might you be filtering out because it's inconvenient, unfamiliar, or too costly to take seriously?

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

I'm overwhelmed. I don't have margin to attend to much of anything right now. +
That's an honest place to begin. The inability to attend is itself a signal worth naming. What is making attention so costly right now?
I can see that things are changing, but I'm not sure what I'm actually looking at. +
That disorientation is appropriate to the moment. This movement isn't asking you to understand yet — just to notice. Stay with what you're seeing before you reach for an explanation.
I've been paying attention, but what I'm noticing troubles me and I don't know what to do with it. +
The move from noticing to action is not the goal here. Sit with what's troubling you a little longer. Name it before you try to fix it.
I'm energized by what I'm seeing and want to move quickly. +
The energy is worth something — hold it. But fast movement from noticing to responding can skip the harder work of understanding what you're actually seeing. Let the attention deepen before you act.
I suspect I'm not seeing what matters most — and I'm not sure how to change that. +
That suspicion is worth trusting. What — or who — might be outside your current frame of attention? This movement invites you to widen before you narrow.
"The five compound drivers are not separate trends to be managed but a single, entangled condition — a landscape that leaders must learn to inhabit, read, and respond to faithfully."
Interpretive Field Guide — Futuring Hub
Scenario

It is 2027. A small church in rural Nova Scotia has lost its third pastor in four years. Its council chair reads about a network of three Halifax churches that recently launched a shared ministry innovation team. She asks: "Why does innovation always seem to happen somewhere else?"

Her question surfaces without self-pity — it is a genuine inquiry into the conditions that make innovation possible or impossible in a given context. What forces are shaping innovation in your context right now — and are you genuinely attending to them?

Reflect
  1. Which of the five compound drivers resonates most strongly in your context right now — ecosystem shifts, diminished imagination, AI and digital change, ambivalence, or constraining instability? What makes it feel most present?
  2. What signals of change related to innovation are you noticing in your ministry context that you haven't yet named out loud? What are you observing at the edges?
  3. Where have you been too busy, too overwhelmed, or too uncertain to attend carefully to what is shaping innovation in your organization? What has that cost you?
Challenge

What You're Not Looking At

The things we avoid attending to rarely go away. They tend to shape us more than the things we face directly.

"Name one signal in your context — something you are aware of, but have been consistently not looking at directly. Why have you been avoiding it? What would it cost you to look?"

Group Practice

Signal Mapping Together

  1. Before the meeting, invite each person to come prepared with one signal they've been noticing — something that feels important but hasn't been named yet in the group.
  2. Go around the table. Each person names their signal in one sentence. No explanation yet. No response from others.
  3. After everyone has shared, invite the group to name what they notice about what was named: Where are the patterns? What is notably absent?
  4. Ask together: What are we attending to well as a leadership team — and what are we systematically not seeing?
  5. Close by identifying one area of shared attention you want to cultivate over the next month.
Micro-Practice

The Slow Look

At the end of each day this week, take five minutes to write down one thing you noticed about your ministry context that surprised you, troubled you, or stayed with you. Don't explain it. Don't fix it. Just name it and move on.

Why it matters

Attention is a practice before it is a skill. You cannot attend to what you haven't noticed, and noticing requires a deliberate slowing that most ministry environments don't naturally create.

Group version

Begin your next team meeting with two minutes of silence, then ask each person to name one thing they've been noticing that they haven't said aloud yet. Listen without responding. Let the room hold it.

Tension

Who Gets to Name the Signal?

Innovation in any context depends on whose perception is treated as reliable information. In most organizations, the people closest to the edges of ministry — the newest staff member, the lay leader in the most under-resourced community, the person who almost left last year — are often holding the most important signals.

They may not be in the room when decisions are made. Or they may be in the room but not asked. Attending faithfully is not just about your own perception. It is about building the conditions under which the right signals can reach the right people.

Notice

What are you noticing about what you notice? Have any of the five drivers surprised you by how present they are? What has been harder to attend to than you expected — and what does that resistance tell you about your organization's relationship with innovation?
