01 Encounter

Two communities. The same shift.
Completely different interpretations.

Read both slowly. Notice where you feel recognition — and where you feel resistance.

Community A — The Boundary Response

The elder board spent two meetings discussing what they were calling "the cafeteria problem" — people picking and choosing from the faith menu rather than committing to the full meal. The solution was a renewed emphasis on membership: clearer expectations, a formal covenant, a membership class that hadn't been required in years. The pastor believed that the church needed to hold the line on what it actually meant to belong.

Attendance stabilized among committed families. But the younger adults — the 20s and early 30s who had been circling the edges of the community — quietly disappeared. A few said privately to friends: "The church made it clear that the way I'm trying to figure this out isn't welcome there." The pastor heard this and struggled. He didn't think that was what he'd communicated. But something had been communicated that he hadn't intended.

Community B — The Open Question Response

The pastor had been in ministry for eighteen years. She had watched self-curated spirituality reshape the people around her for over a decade and had stopped treating it as primarily a problem. At a congregational gathering, she said something that surprised some and relieved others: "I think the hunger underneath all of this is real. People are genuinely searching. I don't think our job is to defend the tradition from people who are searching. I think our job is to be the kind of community worth searching toward."

She started hosting monthly "open table" evenings — not seeker services or programs, but honest conversations about faith, doubt, practice, and belonging. People who hadn't been in a church in years started coming. Some stayed. Many didn't. The pastor called it "holding the door open without pretending there's no threshold."

03 Reframe

Two orientations. One shift. Very different responses.

The report identifies a central interpretive fork in how communities respond to self-curated spirituality. These are not simply strategies — they reflect different theological readings of what is happening and what faithfulness requires. Neither is without risk. Read what each actually claims.

Boundary Orientation Faithfulness as Clarity

The church's response to spiritual fluidity is to articulate and hold its theological boundaries more clearly. Self-curation is a symptom of doctrinal confusion and insufficient formation. The community's calling is to offer clear truth and expect genuine commitment in return. Boundaries are an act of love.

The risk: Boundaries enforced without relationship communicate rejection rather than invitation. Communities that prioritize clarity over curiosity often find they have preserved the doctrine and lost the people — particularly those in genuine searching who needed a hospitable threshold.

Hospitality Orientation Faithfulness as Welcome

The church's response to spiritual fluidity is to become a more genuinely hospitable community — one that makes room for searching, doubt, and gradual formation rather than demanding prior theological clarity as the condition of belonging. The community's calling is to be worth searching toward.

The risk: Hospitality without theological substance can produce communities that are warm and welcoming but theologically thin — where belonging is easy but formation is shallow, and the distinctive gift of the gospel becomes indistinguishable from ambient spirituality.

04 Apply

Interpret what is actually happening in your context.

Three prompts, structured to move from observation to theological meaning to honest accountability. Write with specificity — the goal is interpretation, not resolution.

A
What is self-curated spirituality revealing about your community?

Go beneath the presenting pattern. What is the shift toward self-curation exposing about how your community is perceived — its accessibility, its formation culture, its understanding of belonging and authority?

B
What theological conviction most needs to be heard right now?

Not the most familiar conviction, and not the most comfortable one. The theological truth your community most needs to hear — and most needs to believe — in this specific moment of spiritual fluidity and shifting authority.

C
What story is your community telling itself?

Every community has a narrative about what is happening and why. What story is yours telling about self-curated spirituality — and is it faithful to what is actually there, or is it protecting something that needs to be examined?

Challenge

The Interpretation That Keeps You Comfortable

Every interpretive framework protects something. The narratives we use to make sense of self-curated spirituality almost always preserve our sense of what the church is supposed to be — and what we are supposed to do as leaders. The framework that is hardest to question is usually the one doing the most protective work.

"Name the interpretation of self-curated spirituality in your community that, if it were wrong, would require something significant to change — about your community's culture, its formation approach, or the way you understand your own leadership. What makes that interpretation so difficult to question? Who benefits from it remaining unquestioned?"

Micro-Practice — The Curiosity Conversation

This week, have one undefended conversation with someone who is self-curating their spiritual life. Not to explain, persuade, or invite — only to understand. Ask them: What are you looking for spiritually right now? What have you found? What do you wish the church understood about where you are? Then listen. Write down what you heard.

Why it matters

Most interpretations of self-curated spirituality are built without sustained, curious contact with the people who are actually doing it. The gap between what leaders think is happening and what is actually happening in people's spiritual lives is often significant.

Group version

Each person in the leadership group names the interpretive framework they bring — honestly. Then ask: If someone who was self-curating their spirituality were in the room, what would they say about how we are seeing them? How accurate would our interpretation be?

Tension

The Theology That Spiritual Fluidity Requires

Theology developed inside stable, high-belonging communities tends to assume conditions that no longer obtain. Its instincts are often toward boundary clarity, doctrinal coherence, and community accountability — all of which remain important. But a theology formed in institutional stability can be inadequate to a season of genuine spiritual fluidity, not because it is wrong, but because it was formed in conditions that have changed.

The church has always had to re-engage its inheritance in light of new contexts. The question is not whether the tradition has resources for this moment — it does. The question is whether those resources can be accessed with enough freedom to let self-curated spirituality actually challenge what we believe, rather than simply confirm it.

For teams and leadership groups — Interpretation in Community +
  1. Read the two encounter scenarios as a group. Ask each person: Which community do we more closely resemble — and what do we think about that?
  2. Each person names in one word the emotional register they bring to self-curated spirituality. Write them all where everyone can see them. Notice the range.
  3. Ask together: What theological conviction is most active in how we interpret what is happening? Is it adequate to what we're actually experiencing? What might it be missing?
  4. Invite someone to name the perspective your leadership team doesn't have — a voice that, if present, would challenge how you see this. Make a concrete plan to hear that voice.
  5. Name together: Are we operating from a boundary orientation or a hospitality orientation? What would it mean to hold both with integrity?
