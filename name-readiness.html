05 Movement 05 — Futures of Innovation

Name Readiness

Honestly assessing what innovation your context can receive and sustain

Orientation

Honesty about capacity is not the opposite of faith — it is one of its disciplines. This movement asks you to name, without apology or performance, what your community can actually receive right now. Not what you hope it could receive. Not what it received five years ago when the conditions were different. This kind of honest naming often feels like failure. It is not. It is the necessary starting point for innovation that is sustainable, faithful, and genuinely rooted in the people and communities you are called to serve.

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

I know we need to change, but I honestly don't think we have the capacity right now. +
That's a hard thing to say and an important thing to know. What would it mean to let that honest assessment shape your next decision — rather than quietly override it?
I think I'm ready, but I'm not sure the community around me is. +
That gap between individual readiness and communal readiness is one of the most common sources of innovation strain. What would it take to build readiness in the community — not just to act on your own?
We've been talking about readiness for a long time and I'm not sure we're any closer. +
Long conversations about readiness without movement can become their own kind of avoidance. What specifically is keeping readiness from developing? Is it a real constraint, a decision that hasn't been made, or a conversation that hasn't yet been had honestly?
I feel ready, but I keep second-guessing whether my sense of readiness is accurate. +
That self-questioning can be healthy. What evidence would you need — from your context, from the people you lead, from your own capacity — to trust your sense of readiness?
I'm afraid that naming our limits honestly will be heard as giving up. +
That fear is worth naming directly to the people around you. Honest capacity naming is an act of care for the community — it protects people from being asked to carry more than they can sustain.
"Honest capacity discernment is a necessary condition for sustainable innovation. Organizations that pursue innovation without attending to absorptive capacity — the ability to receive and integrate new ideas without harm — risk collapse or backlash."
Interpretive Field Guide — Futuring Hub
Reflect
  1. The field guide asks: "What kind of innovation are we not resourced to receive right now — emotionally, financially, relationally, culturally?" Name that honestly. What does acknowledging this boundary make possible?
  2. Where in your organization is innovation clustered around one or two individuals with sufficient resilience and support to experiment? What happens to innovation if those people leave or burn out?
  3. What limits in your context are real — and which are assumed? What would you need to test or question to find out?
Challenge

The Innovation You Cannot Receive

Naming a limit is not the same as accepting it as permanent. But it is the only place from which honest movement can begin.

"Name specifically — not generally — what kind of innovation your community is not resourced to receive right now. Not what you don't want. What you genuinely do not have the capacity, margin, or trust to sustain. What does naming that honestly make possible?"

Group Practice

Honest Readiness Conversation

  1. Open with this frame: "This conversation is about honesty, not about deciding. Nothing we name here commits us to a course of action."
  2. Each person completes three sentences in writing, privately: "I am ready to..." / "I am not yet ready to..." / "I am willing to become ready to..."
  3. Share in round-robin. Listen without fixing or reassuring.
  4. The group names together: Where is our collective readiness strongest? Where is the most significant gap?
  5. Identify one next step — small, specific, and doable — that honors both the readiness and the limits you've named.
Micro-Practice

The Readiness Map

On a single page, create three columns: What we genuinely have right now. What we are honestly missing. What we are willing to try in the next thirty days. Keep it short — one to three items per column. Share it with one other person before the week is out.

Why it matters

Most conversations about readiness stay at the level of sentiment. Making it concrete — even briefly — creates conditions for honest discernment rather than wishful thinking or spiraling anxiety.

Group version

Do the mapping together as a leadership team. Build the columns collaboratively. Notice where agreement is easy and where it is hard — the hard places are often where the most important discernment is needed.

Tension

The Myth of the Ready Moment

There is almost never a moment when a community is fully ready for significant innovation. Readiness is not a threshold you cross — it is a condition you cultivate, often imperfectly and unevenly.

The danger is not that communities are unready. It is that they either wait for a readiness that never arrives, or they launch innovation that exceeds what the community can actually absorb — and extract rather than renew.

The question this movement is really asking is not "are we ready?" It is: what kind of readiness are we building — and is it honest?

Notice

What has naming your readiness honestly revealed — about your organization, your leadership, or the conditions you're working within? Did you discover more readiness than you expected, or less? What does "adaptive realism" look like in your context — and how is it different from giving up?
