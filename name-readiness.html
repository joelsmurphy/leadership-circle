01 Encounter

An honest conversation about what
a community can and cannot carry.

Read the fragment below. It is not about failure — it is about the kind of honesty that makes genuine movement possible. Notice what it stirs.

Conversation Fragment — Leadership Retreat

It was the last session of the weekend, and the room had gone quiet in a way that felt different from tiredness. The pastor had just said what everyone had been thinking: "We believe this matters. We've said we want to do it. But I'm not sure we can carry it — not right now. And I don't think we've been honest enough about that."

The finance chair said: "We have the vision. We don't have the margin." The associate pastor said: "I think we have the margin. We don't have the trust." The lay leader in the corner said: "We have both. I'm not sure we have the will."

Nobody argued with any of them. That was the part that stayed with the pastor on the drive home — not that they disagreed, but that they were all right, and none of them had been saying it out loud until now.

Readiness is not a single thing. It is a set of conditions — some present, some absent, some in process. Naming it honestly is not a defeat. It is the beginning of actual preparation.

03 Reframe

Readiness is not one thing.

Select the dimensions where you sense the most significant constraint — or the most underacknowledged strength. You will work with these in the next step.

Emotional
Capacity for change, grief, and sustained uncertainty. Can people absorb what this will require of them personally?
Financial
Resources available to fund exploration, failure, and transition. Does the margin exist — or does it need to be created first?
Relational
The trust, cohesion, and conflict tolerance of the community. Can relationships hold the weight of change without fracturing?
Cultural
The community's appetite for experimentation, tolerance of ambiguity, and relationship to failure. What does the culture actually reward?
Spiritual
The depth and direction of the community's active discernment. Is there genuine attentiveness to the Spirit's movement — or assumption?

04 Apply

Build your readiness map.

For the imagination you've been carrying from the previous movement — or the most significant change you're currently considering — work across these four columns. Be specific and honest in each one.

Strength

What genuine capacity do we bring? Where are we actually more ready than we admit?

Constraint

What is genuinely limiting us? Name real constraints, not diplomatic ones.

Risk

What could go wrong — and what would be the cost? Name the risks we're not talking about.

Condition Needed

What would need to be true — or built — before we could move forward faithfully?

Challenge

The Readiness You've Been Waiting For

Many organizations wait for a readiness that never fully arrives. The conditions are never perfect. The margin is never quite sufficient. The trust is never complete enough. And so the waiting becomes its own kind of decision — one that is rarely named as such.

"How long have you been waiting for readiness — and what has the waiting cost? Is the thing you're waiting for something that can be prepared for, or is it something that only arrives in the act of moving?"

05 Integrate

Name your readiness honestly.

Not a conclusion — a map. Three categories, held without judgment. This artifact carries forward into Next Steps and Learn.

Readiness Map — Futures of Innovation
Ready Now

What can we begin — or continue — without waiting for more conditions to be met? Where is there genuine readiness that we're not fully claiming?

Not Ready Yet

Where do we genuinely need more preparation, more trust, more time, or more resources before moving? Name it without shame.

Needs Attention

What is quietly deteriorating, being avoided, or quietly consuming capacity? What requires attention regardless of what we decide to pursue?

For teams and leadership groups — Shared Readiness Assessment +
  1. Invite each person to complete the individual readiness assessment before gathering. Bring the maps to the table.
  2. Compare: Where do people see the same strengths and constraints? Where do they differ — and what does the difference reveal?
  3. Name together what the group is systematically overestimating — and what it is underestimating.
  4. Work the four readiness map columns together: Where does the group agree? Where is there important disagreement?
  5. Close by naming one condition you will work to create together over the next ninety days — and naming who will be accountable to it.
