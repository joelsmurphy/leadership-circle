05 Movement 05 — Futures of Innovation

Name Readiness

Honestly assessing what innovation your context can receive and sustain

Orientation

The field guide identifies two distinct forces that limit readiness for innovation. The first is external: Constraining Instability — the economic, demographic, political, and institutional pressures that reduce margin and increase risk, making innovation more fragile, cautious, and unevenly distributed. The second is internal: Diminished Imagination — the way chronic fatigue, vocational strain, and sustained overload erode leaders' capacity to imagine, decide, and act, even when they recognize the need for change.

The field guide is clear: acknowledging limits is not a failure of faith. Honest capacity discernment is a necessary condition for sustainable innovation. Organizations that pursue innovation without attending to absorptive capacity — the ability to receive and integrate new ideas without disruption or harm — risk collapse or backlash. And leaders who pretend they have more margin than they do risk the very people and communities they are trying to serve.

This movement invites a courageous kind of honesty — not self-criticism, but clear-eyed naming of where you actually are, which is the only place you can move from.

Reflect
  1. The field guide asks: "What kind of innovation are we not resourced to receive right now — emotionally, financially, relationally, culturally?" Name that honestly. What does acknowledging this boundary make possible?
  2. Where in your organization is innovation clustered around one or two individuals with sufficient resilience and support to experiment? What happens to innovation if those people leave or burn out?
  3. What limits in your context are real — and which are assumed? What would you need to test or question to find out?
Go Deeper

What would you need to release — an initiative, an assumption, a way of measuring success — to create genuine margin for the kind of innovation you've been imagining?

In Conversation

Name your readiness honestly with a trusted peer or leadership colleague. Use this frame from the field guide:

"I am ready to..."  /  "I am not yet ready to..."  /  "I am willing to become ready to..."

Then ask each other: "What would support you in the places you named?" Listen without fixing. The naming itself is the practice.

Practice

For one area of innovation you are holding, create a simple readiness map using three columns:

What we have — existing strengths, relationships, resources, and goodwill that are genuinely available right now.

What we need — what is genuinely missing that would make faithful innovation more possible and sustainable.

What we are willing to do — one or two concrete steps your community is actually prepared to take in the next thirty days.

Keep it short. One page. The goal is clarity, not comprehensiveness.

Notice

After sitting with this movement, reflect:

What has naming your readiness honestly revealed — about your organization, your leadership, or the conditions you're working within? Did you discover more readiness than you expected, or less? What does the field guide call "adaptive realism" look like in your context — and how is it different from giving up?
