Next Steps

Five practice pathways from sensemaking toward faithful, bounded response

Orientation

This is not a program. It is not a strategy. And it is not a way of measuring how well you have engaged the circle. Next Steps is a space for what the Interpretive Field Guide calls a "possible response" — not the right answer to the problem of innovation, but one specific, testable, meaningful step that emerges from genuine discernment.

The five practice pathways below are not ranked. They are not sequential. They are invitations — each suited to a different starting point, a different constraint, or a different form of readiness. Whatever pathway you choose: name it concretely, share it with someone, and be willing to be asked about it thirty days from now.

01
Try Something Small
Run a contained experiment in the next thirty days

Most innovation stalls not at the idea stage but at the threshold of a first actual attempt. This pathway is about crossing that threshold — not with a strategic initiative, but with one specific, bounded, reversible experiment. The goal is not to succeed. It is to generate honest information and to practice the organizational reflex of trying. Most meaningful change begins with something that looked almost embarrassingly small at the start.

Honest Question

What are you calling "not ready yet" that is actually fear of being wrong in public?

Choose one ministry practice, approach, or experiment you've been holding back on. Make it specific, reversible, and time-bounded — thirty days or less. Run it. Before you begin, write down: what you hope to learn, what you're afraid of, and how you will evaluate it honestly afterward. Not whether it "worked." What you learned.

Group version: Name the experiment to your team before you run it. Invite one or two people to co-design it with you. Agree in advance on what honest evaluation will look like — and who will ask the hard questions when it's over.

Carry Forward

After thirty days: what did you learn that you could not have learned without trying? What would you do differently next time — and does "differently" mean adjusting, stopping, or going further?

02
Start a Conversation
Bring someone unexpected into your circle of discernment

Change often stalls not because of a lack of ideas but because the right conversation hasn't happened yet. This pathway invites you to identify one conversation you've been avoiding, deferring, or not even recognizing as necessary — and to begin it. The field guide's ecosystem driver is relevant here: change that stays inside one organization's interpretive framework tends to reproduce what already exists.

Honest Question

Who has a perspective on your ministry's future that you have been treating as a threat rather than as information?

Identify one person whose perspective is genuinely different from yours — in generation, cultural location, organizational role, or theological tradition — and invite a single conversation specifically about the future of your ministry. Come as a learner, not a decision-maker. Ask more than you answer. Do not pitch. Do not defend. Listen for what surprises you.

Group version: Invite two or three such voices into a facilitated conversation with your leadership team. Frame it explicitly as a listening session, not a consultation or a vote. Let the guests speak first.

Carry Forward

After the conversation: what did you hear that you couldn't have generated from within your current circle? What does it require of you to take it seriously?

03
Pay Attention Differently
Build a new noticing practice for the next thirty days

What you attend to shapes what you think is possible. If your information diet, your meeting rhythms, and your daily patterns are producing the same picture of reality over and over, your sense of what is possible will narrow accordingly. This pathway builds a new noticing practice — small enough to sustain, specific enough to generate real insight. You are not gathering information. You are expanding what you are able to see.

Honest Question

What are the sources that most shape your sense of what's possible in ministry — and how genuinely diverse are they?

For four weeks, add one new source of attention to your regular practice. This might be: a weekly conversation with someone outside your sector, a simple signal log (three observations per week about your ministry context), or a regular walk through a part of your community you don't usually move through. Don't analyze yet. Just notice, and write down what you're seeing.

Group version: Each team member takes a different attention practice for one month. Share what you're noticing at your monthly check-in. Let the group's combined attention build a richer picture than any individual could generate alone.

Carry Forward

After four weeks: what are you seeing now that you couldn't see before? How is it changing what feels possible — or what feels necessary?

04
Strengthen Capacity
Address one specific constraint from your readiness map

Capacity is not just a resource question — it is a formation question. This pathway doesn't ask you to solve a capacity problem. It asks you to name one specific, tractable constraint and take one honest step toward reducing it. The goal is not to become resourced enough to innovate freely. It is to practice honest engagement with real limits — and to discover that limits, honestly engaged, are often more workable than they seemed.

Honest Question

What constraint are you managing around instead of addressing directly — and what would it cost you to address it?

Return to your readiness map — from the Name Readiness movement — or create a simple version now. Choose one constraint that feels genuinely addressable in the next ninety days. Not the most urgent — the most tractable. Name one specific action, conversation, or decision that would reduce it. Take that step in the next thirty days.

Group version: Do the readiness map as a team. Let the group name its constraints collectively, then choose together which one to address first. Shared naming of limits is itself a form of capacity building — it reduces the isolation of individual leaders carrying what should be a communal concern.

Carry Forward

Ninety days from now: has the constraint shifted — even slightly? What did you learn from the attempt, about the constraint and about how you and your community respond to honest limits?

05
Let Something Go
Create margin through intentional release

The field guide identifies diminished imagination as one of the most significant barriers to faithful innovation — and diminished imagination is almost always a capacity problem. You cannot restore capacity without releasing something. This pathway takes that seriously. It asks not what you should add, but what you might finally be ready to lay down. Release is not failure. In many contexts, it is the most innovative and faithful thing a leader can do.

Honest Question

What are you holding onto that is costing more than it is contributing — and what story are you telling yourself that makes it feel non-negotiable?

Name one thing — an initiative, a practice, an assumption, a role, a way of measuring success — that you are willing to hold more loosely. Do not begin by announcing an ending. Take one step in the direction of release: have a conversation with a key stakeholder, reduce your personal investment by one deliberate degree, or write a simple transition sketch. Let that step be the beginning of discernment, not a unilateral decision.

Group version: Invite your leadership group to name collectively one thing the organization is holding that may be preventing more important things from growing. Discern together whether this is the right season to release it — and what a faithful, caring process of release would look like.

Carry Forward

After you've taken one step toward release: does it feel like loss, relief, or both? What has that response revealed about what was actually at stake — and what might be possible in the space the release creates?

A Final Word

The field guide's closing sentence is an invitation as much as a conclusion: "The task ahead is not to control the future, but to become capable of receiving it — faithfully, wisely, and together."

Next Steps is not a graduation. It is a beginning of a different kind of practice — one that is less about managing innovation and more about being formed, over time, into the kind of leader and community that can inhabit the future faithfully.

Whatever step you take from here: name it, share it, and be willing to be asked about it.
