Next Steps

Five practice pathways from sensemaking toward faithful, bounded response

Orientation

This is not a program. It is not a strategy. And it is not a way of measuring how well you have engaged the circle. Next Steps is a space for what the Interpretive Field Guide calls a "possible response" — not the right answer to the problem of innovation, but one specific, testable, meaningful step that emerges from genuine discernment.

The five practice pathways below are not ranked. They are not sequential. They are invitations — each one suited to a different starting point, a different constraint, or a different form of readiness. You may find yourself drawn to one immediately. You may want to sit with the circle for a while before choosing. That is entirely appropriate.

Whatever pathway you choose, the field guide's counsel holds: name it concretely, share it with someone, and be willing to be asked about it thirty days from now.

01
Try Something Small
Run a contained experiment in the next thirty days

One of the most effective antidotes to innovation paralysis is a small, bounded experiment — something you can learn from without betting the community on it. The goal is not to succeed. It is to generate new information, practice honest evaluation, and build the organizational reflex for experimentation. Most meaningful innovation begins with something that looked almost embarrassingly small at the start.

Tangible Step

Choose one thing you've been considering changing or trying. Make it specific, time-limited, and reversible. Run it. Then evaluate honestly — with someone who will ask hard questions.

With Whom?

Identify two or three people who will join you in the experiment and commit to honest reflection afterward. Do not proceed alone.

How Will You Know?

Before you begin, name what you would need to see or hear to know this was worth doing — or not worth doing again. Write it down.

02
Start a Conversation
Bring someone unexpected into your circle of discernment

Innovation often stalls not because of capacity, but because of perspective. Whose voice is missing from your current discernment? Whose experience of the forces shaping your context is different from your own — and therefore capable of seeing what you cannot? The ecosystem driver in the field guide identifies this as a structural issue: change that stays inside one organization's interpretive framework tends to reproduce what already exists.

Tangible Step

Identify one person from outside your usual circle — a different generation, tradition, cultural context, or sector — and invite a single, focused conversation about the future of your ministry.

With Whom?

Go alone, or with one other person. Don't bring the full team. Give the conversation space to be surprising — and give yourself permission to be changed by it.

How Will You Know?

What would this person need to say to shift how you're thinking? Name that threshold before you go. Notice if it gets crossed.

03
Pay Attention Differently
Build a new noticing practice for the next thirty days

The Attend movement asks what you are already noticing about the forces shaping innovation in your context. This pathway invites you to build a regular, intentional practice of attending — not to strategy, but to signals of change that you might otherwise filter out. Perception shapes possibility. Leaders who develop richer noticing practices tend to have access to a wider range of possible responses.

Tangible Step

For thirty days, keep a simple signal log. Record one observation per week — a moment, conversation, or decision where you noticed one of the five compound drivers at work. Don't analyze. Just notice and name.

With Whom?

Share your log with one other person every two weeks. Ask: what does this reveal about what we're paying attention to — and what we might be filtering out?

How Will You Know?

After thirty days, review the log. What surprised you? What patterns emerged that you did not expect? Let those surprises inform what you attend to next.

04
Strengthen Capacity
Address one specific constraint from your readiness map

The Name Readiness movement invites you to be honest about what kind of innovation your context can actually receive. This pathway takes the next step: not lamenting the constraint, but asking what is genuinely tractable. Not the most urgent constraint — the most addressable one. The goal is not to solve the problem but to reduce it, and to learn from the attempt.

Tangible Step

Return to your readiness map (or create one now using the Name Readiness practice). Choose the constraint that feels most addressable in the next ninety days. Name one concrete step toward reducing it.

With Whom?

Take this step with the people who hold the constraint with you — not as a top-down initiative, but as shared discernment about what is genuinely possible together.

How Will You Know?

Ninety days from now: has the constraint shifted? Even slightly? What did you learn from trying — about the constraint, and about how your community responds to honest naming?

05
Let Something Go
Create margin through intentional release

The field guide identifies diminished imagination as one of the most significant barriers to faithful innovation — and diminished imagination is almost always a capacity problem. It requires release: of an initiative that is no longer life-giving, an assumption that is no longer accurate, a way of measuring success that is quietly shaping everything, or a form of ministry held more by obligation than by genuine call. Release is not failure. In many contexts, it is the most innovative thing a leader can do.

Tangible Step

Name one thing you are currently holding that, if released, would create genuine margin for the kind of innovation you have been imagining. Then ask: what would it actually take to let it go?

With Whom?

This is a conversation for your leadership community — not a unilateral decision. Name what you are considering releasing, and invite honest discernment about whether this is the right time and the right way.

How Will You Know?

Does naming it feel like relief, grief, or both? That response is data. And: six months from now, what has the release made room for that wasn't there before?

A Final Word

The field guide's closing sentence is an invitation as much as a conclusion: "The task ahead is not to control the future, but to become capable of receiving it — faithfully, wisely, and together."

Next Steps is not a graduation. It is a beginning of a different kind of practice — one that is less about managing innovation and more about being formed, over time, into the kind of leader and community that can inhabit the future faithfully.

Whatever step you take from here: name it, share it, and be willing to be asked about it.
