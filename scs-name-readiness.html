01 Encounter

The vision was real.
The gap between vision and capacity was also real.

Read this slowly. Notice what it names — and what it makes you want to avoid naming about your own community.

Conversation Fragment — Staff Retreat, 2025

The pastor had just finished sharing her vision: a community with a permeable edge and a theologically rich centre, genuinely hospitable to people in deconstruction, forming faith in relationship rather than through membership class. The team had been moved by it. It felt like the right direction.

Then the associate pastor said something quietly: "I believe every word of that. And I want to name something that I think we need to talk about. Most of our leaders have been formed entirely within our tradition. They're not sure how to sit with someone who's deconstructing without feeling like they need to fix them. Our formation culture is built around answers, not around holding questions. We have the vision. I'm not sure we have the capacity yet."

There was a long pause. The pastor said: "That's honest. And I think you're right. So where do we start?"

Readiness names the gap between the vision and the capacity — not to abandon the vision, but to build toward it with honesty.

03 Reframe

Readiness for self-curated spirituality has multiple dimensions.

Engaging a self-curating spiritual culture is not primarily a program or communications challenge. It requires capacity across five dimensions of community life. Select the ones most relevant to your context right now.

Relational Capacity
The depth and quality of relationships your community can offer people who are spiritually searching — the ability to sit with people mid-deconstruction without needing to fix, convince, or close the conversation
Theological Capacity
The community's ability to engage theological questions honestly and with depth — neither collapsing into vague spirituality nor defending tradition in ways that shut down genuine inquiry
Adaptive Leadership
The leader's capacity to function in decentralized, participatory, and fluid community structures — to lead relationally rather than positionally, and to form faith alongside people rather than above them
Digital Awareness
The community's understanding of how digital and algorithmic environments are shaping the people it seeks to serve — and its capacity to engage those environments with theological wisdom rather than avoidance or uncritical adoption
Communal Maturity
The community's capacity to hold ambiguity, welcome difference, and sustain genuine belonging without requiring uniform belief as the condition of participation — the emotional and spiritual resilience that hospitable formation requires

04 Apply

Map your community's readiness.

Four columns. Not a performance review — an honest accounting. Take time with each. The goal is a clear-eyed picture, not a damning verdict or a reassuring one.

Strength

What genuine capacity does your community have right now for engaging people who are self-curating their spirituality — something you can actually rely on?

Constraint

What is genuinely limiting your community's capacity to engage a self-curating culture — a real constraint, not a problem you can solve with one new program?

Risk

What could break down — theologically, relationally, or institutionally — if your community moves toward greater hospitality without the formation to sustain it?

Condition Needed

What would need to be true — about your leaders' formation, your community's culture, or your theological practices — to move faithfully toward the vision you named in Imagine?

Challenge

The Formation Your Leaders Haven't Had

The associate pastor in the encounter scenario named something most communities avoid saying out loud: the leaders themselves may not have been formed for the kind of engagement a self-curating culture requires. Leaders formed in high-certainty, high-belonging communities often struggle to sit with searching, hold questions without resolving them, and build relationships that don't move toward explicit commitment.

"Name one formation gap in your leadership team — not a knowledge gap, but a relational or spiritual capacity gap — that would need to be addressed before your community could genuinely engage people who are mid-deconstruction or spiritually searching. What would it actually take to address it? Has anyone in your community ever named this gap before?"

Micro-Practice — The Honest Inventory

This week, have a fifteen-minute conversation with one other leader in your community using two questions only: What do we genuinely have to offer someone who is building their faith outside any tradition? and What would we genuinely struggle to offer them? Write down the answers before you leave the conversation. Notice the gap between what you publicly claim and what the honest inventory reveals.

Why it matters

Communities that overstate their relational or theological readiness tend to produce encounters with searching people that feel welcoming on the surface and subtly unwelcoming beneath it. Honest assessment is a form of care — for the community and for the people it hopes to serve.

Group version

Present the Readiness Map to your leadership team. Each person fills it out independently, then the group shares and compares. The places of significant divergence — where one person sees a strength and another sees a constraint — are where the most important conversations are waiting.

Tension

Readiness Does Not Precede the Call

The biblical pattern is not that communities become ready and then receive their calling. It is that communities are called into readiness through faithful response. The disciples were sent before they were equipped. The early church was formed by its mission, not before it. Readiness assessment is not a gate that determines whether you act — it is a map that shapes how you act and what you need to build along the way.

What this means practically is that naming your community's gaps honestly is not an excuse to wait. It is the beginning of a more honest and sustainable engagement. The community in the encounter scenario didn't abandon its vision when the associate pastor named the capacity gap. It asked: where do we start? That question, taken seriously, is the beginning of genuine readiness.

For teams and leadership groups — Naming Readiness Together +
  1. Read the encounter scenario together. Ask: Has our team ever had that conversation — the honest one about whether we actually have what the vision requires? If not, name why not.
  2. Each person completes the Readiness Map independently, then shares one item from each column. Notice where the group's assessments converge and where they diverge significantly.
  3. Together, name the dimension of readiness your community is most consistently avoiding discussing — and why that conversation is being avoided.
  4. Ask: What is one formation investment — for the leadership team, not for the congregation — that would most increase your community's actual capacity to engage self-curated spirituality? Make a concrete plan to pursue it.
  5. Close with each person naming one honest thing about your community's readiness they commit to holding without minimizing or catastrophizing over the next six months.
