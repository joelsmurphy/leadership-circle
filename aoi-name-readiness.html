01 Encounter

The honest conversation
most leadership teams avoid.

Read this fragment slowly. Notice what it names — and what it makes you want to avoid naming.

Conversation Fragment — Leadership Retreat, 2025

The lead pastor had been talking for fifteen minutes about what the community could become — a wider presence in the neighbourhood, deeper intersectoral partnerships, a model of trauma-informed ministry. She was good at this. The vision was real.

Then the chair of the board asked the question that changed the room: "I believe all of that. But I want to ask something harder. Do we have what it would actually take? Not what we want to have — what we actually have, right now, with these people, this budget, this level of energy in the room?"

There was a long silence. Then the associate pastor said something that nobody had said before in a formal meeting: "Honestly? I don't think we do. I think we're running on fumes. And I think the vision is real, and I think we're not ready for it. And I don't know what to do with both of those things being true at the same time."

Readiness in instability is not pessimism. It is the refusal to let vision substitute for honest assessment — and the courage to hold both simultaneously.

03 Reframe

Adaptive capacity has multiple dimensions.

Readiness for navigating instability is not primarily a financial question — though financial capacity matters. It is shaped by five dimensions of community life. Select the ones where your community's capacity is most relevant to the instability you're facing.

Emotional
The community's collective capacity to absorb grief, anxiety, and uncertainty without fragmentation — the emotional bandwidth available for navigating disruption together
Relational
The depth and breadth of trust within the community — between members, between leadership and congregation, and between the community and its neighbours and partners
Adaptive Capacity
The community's actual track record of changing, releasing things that no longer work, and learning from disruption — not just its stated openness to change
Spiritual
The depth of the community's spiritual formation — whether the community has the theological and contemplative resources to hold uncertainty, lament, and ambiguity without collapsing into either denial or despair
Organizational
The community's structural and financial capacity — staff, budget, decision-making processes, and institutional flexibility — to absorb change without losing operational stability

04 Apply

Map your community's instability readiness.

Four columns. Not a performance review — an honest accounting. Take time with each. The goal is a clear-eyed picture, not a damning verdict.

Strength

What genuine adaptive capacity does your community have right now — something you can actually rely on in a season of sustained instability?

Constraint

What is limiting your community's capacity to navigate instability right now — not a problem you can solve quickly, but a real constraint on what is possible?

Risk

What could break down in your community if instability intensifies? Not a catastrophic projection — a realistic concern about where your adaptive capacity is thinnest?

Condition Needed

What would need to be true — about your community's spiritual formation, relational trust, structural capacity, or leadership — for you to navigate instability more faithfully?

Challenge

The Capacity You're Not Counting

Most readiness assessments focus on what is absent — what the community lacks, what it can't do, what it has lost. But in seasons of instability, communities often have latent capacity that has not yet been named or activated: people who have navigated personal disruption, relationships with neighbours that haven't been leveraged, spiritual resources that haven't been mobilized, organizational flexibility that hasn't been tested.

"Name one capacity your community has that is not yet being counted in how your leadership thinks about readiness. It may be a person, a relationship, a spiritual practice, an organizational form, or something else entirely. Why hasn't it been counted? What would it mean to count it?"

Micro-Practice — The Honest Inventory

This week, have a fifteen-minute conversation with one other person in your community's leadership — using only two questions: What do we genuinely have? and What do we genuinely not have? Write down the answers before you leave the conversation. Notice the gap between what you're publicly claiming about your community's readiness and what the honest inventory reveals.

Why it matters

Communities that overstate their readiness tend to take on more than they can carry — and the gap between promise and capacity becomes a crisis rather than a managed constraint. Honest assessment is a form of care for the community.

Group version

Present the Readiness Map to your leadership team. Ask each person to fill it out independently, then compare. Notice where assessments converge and diverge — the divergences are where the most important conversations live.

Tension

Readiness Is Not a Prerequisite for Faithfulness

One of the traps of readiness assessment is the assumption that the community must be ready before it can respond faithfully. But the biblical pattern is often the reverse: communities are called into readiness through response, not before it. The disciples were not ready when they were sent. The prophets were not equipped when they were called. Readiness and faithfulness are not sequential — they develop together.

What this means practically is that the readiness assessment is not a gate. It is a map. Knowing what your community has and lacks doesn't determine whether you act — it shapes how you act and what kind of support you seek. Faithful response often begins not with full capacity but with honest acknowledgment of what is available, and the decision to act from that place rather than waiting for conditions that may not arrive.

For teams and leadership groups — Naming Readiness Together +
  1. Read the encounter scenario together. Ask: Has our team ever had this conversation — the honest one about whether we have what this actually requires? If not, name why not.
  2. Each person completes the Readiness Map independently, then shares one item from each column. Notice where assessments converge and where they diverge significantly.
  3. Together, identify the dimension of adaptive capacity your community is most avoiding discussing — and name why it is being avoided.
  4. Ask together: What is one latent capacity in our community that we have not yet counted? Make a concrete plan to activate or invest in it.
  5. Close with each person naming one honest thing about the community's current readiness that they commit to holding without minimizing or catastrophizing over the next three months.
