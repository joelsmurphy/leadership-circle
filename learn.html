06 Movement 06 — Futures of Innovation

Learn

Becoming capable of receiving the future faithfully, wisely, and together

Orientation

This movement is not a summary. It is a gathering. You are invited to notice what has genuinely shifted — not what you have learned about innovation as a concept, but what has changed in how you see, decide, lead, and relate. Learning in this sense is not the acquisition of new information. It is the integration of experience over time — what the field guide calls "becoming capable of receiving the future." That capacity does not arrive fully formed. It is practiced, tested, and deepened in real contexts, with real people, over time.

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

Something has genuinely shifted in how I see this, and I want to name it. +
Name it — even tentatively. The act of articulating a shift is part of how it becomes stable enough to carry forward and act on.
I've engaged the material but I'm not sure anything has really changed. +
That's an honest thing to notice. What were you hoping would change? What would it take for this engagement to have been genuinely useful to you?
I have more questions now than I did when I started. +
That's often a sign that something real has happened. What are the most important questions you're carrying? Are they questions you're willing to sit with for a season — without forcing a quick resolution?
I know what has shifted, but I'm not sure what to do with it. +
The gap between insight and action is normal — and worth honoring rather than rushing past. What would it mean to carry the insight forward without immediately converting it into a plan?
I'm aware that what I've been learning needs to be shared with the people I lead. +
That awareness matters. How you carry learning back into a community shapes what it does there. What would it look like to share not just what you've concluded, but what you've experienced?

  2. The field guide asks: "What fears are quietly shaping your approach to innovation more than hope?" Having sat with this material, what is your honest answer? What are you most afraid of losing if you don't innovate — and most afraid of risking if you do?

Challenge

The Gap Between Insight and Change

Learning that changes nothing is not yet formation. Formation shows up in behavior, relationship, and decision over time — not in what we can articulate, but in what we actually do.

"You may have learned something here. But have you changed anything? What would be different — in a meeting, a decision, a conversation, a practice — if this learning were genuinely integrated into how you lead?"

Group Practice

Shared Integration Circle

  1. Gather the group. Open with two minutes of quiet.
  2. Each person completes and shares: "The most honest thing I can say about what has shifted for me is..."
  3. After all have shared, the group names together: What are we carrying collectively that we didn't have before?
  4. Ask: What do we need to release — an assumption, a practice, a priority — to make room for what we're learning?
  5. Close by identifying one concrete, shared change you will make in the next ninety days that reflects what this circle has opened.
Micro-Practice

The Three-Sentence Integration

Write three sentences — not more — in response to this sequence: One thing that has shifted for me is... One question I'm still carrying is... One conversation I need to have in the next thirty days is...

Why it matters

Integration without articulation tends to dissipate. Three sentences is small enough to do honestly and specific enough to be useful. The constraint matters — resist the urge to expand it.

Group version

Begin your next team meeting with five minutes where each person shares their three sentences. Listen without debating. Close by naming what the group wants to carry forward together.

Commentary

What Formation Actually Takes

The field guide ends with a sentence that is easy to misread as encouragement: "The task ahead is not to control the future, but to become capable of receiving it." It sounds hopeful. It is also a description of something that requires years, not sessions.

Becoming capable of receiving the future faithfully means being formed — in community, over time, through failure and honesty and the slow accumulation of practices that change what you notice, how you decide, and who you are willing to listen to.

A circle like this one does not complete that formation. At best, it names what formation requires, interrupts what would have continued unchanged, and points toward the kind of ongoing practice that makes it possible. That is not nothing. But it is a beginning, not an arrival.

Notice

What kind of leader — and what kind of community — is this experience forming you to become? The future of innovation in the Canadian Christian landscape will not be shaped by perfect strategies. It will be shaped by leaders willing to pay attention, to learn from one another, to hold tension well, and to take small, faithful steps forward together. What is your next step?
