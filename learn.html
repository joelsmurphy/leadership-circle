06 Movement 06 — Futures of Innovation

Learn

Becoming capable of receiving the future faithfully, wisely, and together

Orientation

The field guide closes with this: "The task ahead is not to control the future, but to become capable of receiving it — faithfully, wisely, and together." This is the heart of what learning means in this context. Not acquiring more information about innovation. Not completing a formation program. But being genuinely changed — in how you see, how you decide, who you listen to, and what you trust.

The field guide identifies a risk it calls "diminished imagination" — not a lack of ideas, but a narrowing of what leaders believe is possible, driven by fatigue, overload, and fear. Formation that restores imagination is therefore not a luxury. It is a structural condition for faithful innovation. Whether innovation in the coming years is reactive or reflective will depend largely on whether leaders are formed to engage change as a site of discernment rather than as a threat to personal or institutional sustainability.

This final movement is not an ending. It is a turning — toward the communities you lead, toward the futures you've been imagining, toward the one concrete step that is actually possible now.

Reflect
  1. What has most shifted for you through this circle — not what you've learned about innovation, but what has genuinely changed in how you see it, approach it, or lead within it?
  2. The field guide asks: "What fears are quietly shaping your approach to innovation more than hope?" Having sat with this material, what is your honest answer? What are you most afraid of losing if you don't innovate — and most afraid of risking if you do?
  3. Where do you sense the Spirit's invitation in relation to innovation in your context — not as a strategy to implement, but as a posture to inhabit over the next season?
Go Deeper

If engaging this circle were a turning point in how you think about innovation and formation, what would it be a turning point toward — and what would it require you to let go of?

In Conversation

Share with your community, your team, or a trusted circle — not a summary of what you've read, but what it has meant for you:

"Here is what has shifted for me. Here is the question I'm carrying forward that I didn't arrive with. Here is what I think it might mean for how we lead together — and here is what I am still uncertain about."

Invite others to share what has been forming in them too. Let this become a practice of shared reflection, not a presentation from one person.

Practice

Identify one concrete change to make in the next thirty days — grounded in what you've been learning and discerning through this circle. The field guide calls this a "possible response": not a project, not a strategic initiative, but one specific, testable, meaningful step.

Name it: What will you do? With whom? By when? How will you know if it mattered?

Then share it with one person and ask them to check in with you in thirty days.

Notice

Finally, sit with this question — and perhaps return to it in a month, or a season from now:

What kind of leader — and what kind of community — is this experience forming you to become? The future of innovation in the Canadian Christian landscape will not be shaped by perfect strategies. It will be shaped by leaders willing to pay attention, to learn from one another, to hold tension well, and to take small, faithful steps forward together. What is your next step?
