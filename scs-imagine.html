01 Encounter

Communities are already experimenting.
Here is what some of them look like.

These aren't predictions or prescriptions. They are imaginative projections grounded in emerging signals. Read each slowly. Notice what draws you, what makes you uncomfortable, and what stirs something in you.

Snapshot A — 2035 / The Threshold Community

The congregation stopped trying to define who was "in" and started defining what they were about. They meet on Thursday evenings in a converted industrial space — a liturgically grounded, deeply relational gathering that explicitly welcomes people at every stage of belief, deconstruction, and reconstruction. The pastor describes it as "a community with a clear theological centre and a permeable edge." Commitment grows through relationship, not through membership class.

About a third of the regulars would not call themselves Christians, but they keep coming back. The pastor says: "We stopped asking people to decide before they belonged. We let belonging lead to formation."

Snapshot B — 2035 / The Distributed Formation Network

What began as a single congregation is now a loose network of small groups, digital communities, and occasional gathered worship — connected not by a building or a Sunday schedule but by a shared rule of life, mutual accountability, and a monthly in-person gathering. Leaders are trained relationally rather than credentialed institutionally. Formation happens in everyday life, not primarily in programs.

People who work with this network describe their spiritual life as the most coherent it has ever been. They are forming faith in community. The community just doesn't look like what any of them expected.

Snapshot C — 2035 / The Depth Offer

The congregation made a deliberate choice to go deeper rather than broader. It became known in its city for serious theological formation — not as gatekeeping, but as genuine gift. It offers multi-year formation tracks, apprenticeship in contemplative practice, serious engagement with the tradition's intellectual heritage, and honest space for doubt and deconstruction within a theologically rich framework. It is not large. It is deeply formative.

People who are self-curating their spirituality from a dozen different sources sometimes find their way here because they are hungry for the thing the algorithm cannot give them: depth, accountability, and a community that takes formation seriously enough to ask something of them.

03 Reframe

What is orienting your imagination?

Faithful imagination in a self-curating spiritual culture isn't directionless — it is shaped by what your community believes it is for. Three orientations are at work in communities navigating this landscape. Select the one that most honestly describes what is driving yours.

Coherence
The primary imagination is doctrinal: how does the community maintain theological integrity and offer a coherent, integrated faith in a fragmented, self-curating environment? The goal is a life-shaping vision that holds together what self-curation pulls apart. The question is: what does a whole and rooted faith look like from here?

Coherence imagination takes the tradition's gifts seriously — but if it becomes primarily defensive, it can produce communities that are theologically clear but relationally inaccessible to people who are still forming.

Hospitality
The primary imagination is relational: how does the community become genuinely hospitable to people at every stage of spiritual formation — including those mid-deconstruction, hybrid in their practice, or spiritually curious but theologically unsettled? The goal is a permeable threshold and a generous belonging. The question is: who needs us to hold the door open?

Hospitality imagination is often what communities discover is their deepest calling — but without theological substance it can produce warmth without formation, belonging without depth.

Depth
The primary imagination is formational: how does the community offer what algorithms and self-curation cannot — genuine depth, serious formation, honest accountability, and a tradition rich enough to hold both certainty and doubt? The goal is to become worth searching toward. The question is: what does serious formation look like when formation itself is being renegotiated?

Depth imagination can draw people who are hungry for what curated content cannot give — but it requires communities that can hold depth without using it as a barrier to those who are still arriving.

04 Apply

Write your community's 2035 formation snapshot.

Not a strategic plan. An imaginative image — specific, human, grounded in your actual context, honest about the self-curating landscape you're inhabiting. Use the scaffold below to build it.

Imaginative Exercise — 2035 It is 2035. Your community is practicing formation in a world where most people construct their own spiritual lives. Write toward what you can actually see — not what you hope for in the abstract, but what faithful community looks like in your specific context.

01 / The Gathering — Describe a specific moment of community in 2035. Where are you? Who is there — including people who would not have found their way here before? What is happening?

02 / The Formation — How is faith being formed in this community? What practices, relationships, and rhythms are shaping people — and how does this engage rather than retreat from the self-curating culture around it?

03 / What Was Released — What did your community have to let go of — an expectation, a program, a gatekeeping practice, an assumption about what belonging requires — to become this community?

04 / What Was Held — What stayed non-negotiable? What theological conviction or communal practice remained essential through all the change?

Challenge

The Imagination That Stays Safe

Most institutional imagination about self-curated spirituality stays within the frame of what the community can currently manage — a slightly more welcoming version of what already exists, with a few new programs. The snapshots above are not asking for incremental adjustment. They are asking for a genuinely different imagination about what faithful formation community could become.

"Name one thing your community would have to stop being — or stop protecting — in order to become genuinely hospitable to someone who is building their faith entirely outside traditional structures. Not a program you'd add. An assumption you'd have to release. What is that assumption? Who in your community is most invested in keeping it?"

Micro-Practice — The Imagination Conversation

Identify one person in your context who has built a genuinely rich spiritual life outside church — someone who is searching seriously, practicing sincerely, and finding real meaning. Ask if you can spend an hour learning about their spiritual life: not to evaluate it, but to understand it. Then ask yourself: What does their spiritual life have that your community's formation culture doesn't? What does your community have that theirs doesn't? Write down what you notice.

Why it matters

Faithful imagination about self-curated spirituality is not possible without actual contact with the people who are doing it well. Most institutional imagination is built from distance and worry. What it needs is proximity and curiosity.

Group version

Read all three snapshots aloud. After each, invite one word from each person: What does this stir? Then ask together: If one of these communities were our future, what would need to be different about us now? What would we need to release? What would need to become more central?

Tension

What Only Community Can Offer

Self-curated spirituality can produce genuine faith, real formation, and meaningful practice. But there are things it cannot produce: the friction of genuine community, the formation that comes through sustained accountability, the theological depth that builds over generations, the witness of a shared life that stands for something beyond individual preference. These are not things the algorithm can curate. They are what only embodied, committed, theologically rooted community can offer.

The question is not whether the church has something to offer a self-curating spiritual culture. It does. The question is whether the way the church currently offers it makes it accessible — or whether the packaging has become so identified with a particular cultural form that the gift inside is invisible to the people who most need it.

For teams and leadership groups — Imagining Together +
  1. Read the three snapshots aloud. After each one, invite one word from each person. Then together: Which snapshot feels most like a possibility for us — and which feels most threatening to what we currently are?
  2. Each person writes their own 2035 formation snapshot for your community — five minutes, without editing. Read them aloud without commentary. Notice what recurs across them.
  3. Ask together: What imagination stance are we most drawn to — Coherence, Hospitality, or Depth? What does that reveal about what we currently believe our community is for?
  4. Name one assumption about belonging, formation, or membership that the group's imagination consistently protected — something that none of the snapshots challenged. Ask: Why is that assumption so durable? Is it theological, or institutional, or something else?
  5. Close by naming together one imaginative capacity your team wants to cultivate this year — not a program, but a posture or a practice of seeing your context differently.
