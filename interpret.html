03 Frame

Three ideas that may sharpen what you are noticing.

Open any that feel relevant. These are not explanations — they are lenses. Read them against what you surfaced above.

Interpretation is shaped by experience, not just information +

What we have lived through shapes what we think change means. A community that once lost a beloved pastor through a poorly managed transition will interpret a new structural proposal through the lens of that loss — even if the situations are entirely different.

Our interpretations are not neutral. They carry the weight of previous encounters with abandonment, betrayal, hope, or renewal. This is not a problem to fix. It is a condition to understand.

Emotion is interpretive — not just reactive +

Grief is a judgment that something valuable is being lost. Hope is a judgment that something better is possible. Anger is a judgment that something unjust has occurred. The emotional register of a community is not separate from its theology — it is theology in action.

When a leader dismisses emotional responses as "just feelings," they are not being more rational. They are being less interpretively honest. The feelings are telling you what people believe is at stake.

Theological language can open discernment — or foreclose it +

Words like "faithfulness," "mission," "tradition," and "calling" carry enormous weight. They can genuinely clarify what is being asked of a community. They can also serve as protected territory — language used not to think more clearly but to shut down the thinking of others.

The same word used by two people in the same room may mean entirely different things — and may be doing entirely different work. Noticing how theological language is functioning is one of the most important things a leader can do in a moment of change.
