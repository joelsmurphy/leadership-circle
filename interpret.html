02 Movement 02 — Futures of Innovation

Interpret

Making theological meaning of what innovation requires

Orientation

Interpretation is what happens in the gap between noticing and deciding. It is where our assumptions live, where our fears do their quietest work, and where we tell ourselves stories that feel like analysis. This movement asks you to slow that gap down — to look at how you're making meaning, not just what you're concluding. Whose interpretation is shaping the room? What is being protected by the story you're telling? And where is your theological tradition being used to legitimate something that deserves a harder look?

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

I have a pretty clear interpretation of what's happening — I think I know what this means. +
That clarity is worth examining. How long have you held this interpretation? What would have to be true for you to be wrong about it?
I'm holding multiple conflicting interpretations and I don't know how to sort them. +
That's a good place to be, even if it doesn't feel that way. Sit with the conflict a little longer. The tension between interpretations is often where the most important question lives.
I notice my interpretation is shaped more by fear than by careful reflection. +
Naming that is a significant act of honesty. Fear is not always a distortion — sometimes it is a signal. But it is worth asking: what specifically are you afraid of losing?
The people around me have a fundamentally different reading of the situation than I do. +
That difference is important information. This movement invites you to be curious about their interpretation — not to be persuaded by it, but to understand what they're seeing that you may not be.
I'm not sure my interpretation of my context has changed much in a long time. +
That may be appropriate — or it may reflect how much our frameworks can calcify under pressure. What new information have you encountered recently that has genuinely changed how you read your context?
"Innovation may be the wrong word for our impulse to find new forms of ministry and new ways of being the church. The word we are groping for is love: How can we love people well given this new context in which we find ourselves?"
Kendra Creasy Dean — cited in the Interpretive Field Guide
Reflect
  1. How has your theological tradition shaped what you see as legitimate innovation — and what falls outside that frame? Which voices or forms of experimentation does your tradition tend to trust, and which does it overlook?
  2. The field guide identifies "innovation-washing" — adopting innovation language without deeper learning or structural change. Where might this be happening in your context? What makes it difficult to name?
  3. Where do you feel genuine theological tension with the futures being described — between faithfulness and relevance, tradition and change, conviction and openness? What does that tension reveal?
Challenge

What Are You Protecting?

Honest interpretation often requires naming what we are defending, not just what we are discerning.

"Underneath your interpretation of what's happening in your context right now — what are you protecting? A relationship? A way of leading? A sense of what your organization is for? An image of yourself?"

Group Practice

Interpretation Audit

  1. Choose one significant change or challenge your community is currently navigating.
  2. Ask each person in the group to write down individually: "Our dominant interpretation of this situation is..." and "An interpretation we are probably not taking seriously enough is..."
  3. Share both in order, without debating or defending.
  4. Together, identify: whose perspective has been least present in shaping your group's interpretation? How could you bring it in?
  5. Close by naming one assumption your group is willing to hold more loosely going forward.
Micro-Practice

Name the Story

Before your next significant leadership decision, write down privately the story you are telling yourself about why this decision is necessary. Then ask: who benefits from this story? Who does it make invisible? What would a different story — told by someone in a different position — look like?

Why it matters

We are all narrating our contexts, all the time. Making that narration visible is one of the most important things a leader can do — especially when facing pressure to change.

Group version

In a team meeting, ask each person to complete this sentence before discussion begins: "The story I've been telling myself about this situation is..." Share without debate. Notice where the stories converge and where they diverge.

Commentary

Innovation-Washing and the Stories We Tell

The field guide names a pattern it calls "innovation-washing" — adopting the language of innovation without the deeper learning or structural change it requires. It's easy to dismiss as cynicism. But it's worth asking why it happens. Often it is not bad faith. It is relief. When a community is under pressure, calling something "innovation" can provide a temporary sense of momentum — a story that something is happening — without requiring the harder work of honest discernment about what actually needs to change.

The interpretation that needs examining is often not a lie. It is a partial truth doing more work than it should.
