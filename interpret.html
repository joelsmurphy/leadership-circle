02 Movement 02 — Futures of Innovation

Interpret

Making theological meaning of what innovation requires

Orientation

The field guide frames innovation not as technique or strategy, but as faithful change — change that adds value (American Sociological Association, 2013). This framing is deliberately theological. Kendra Creasy Dean writes that innovation may be the wrong word altogether: "The word we are groping for is love: How can we love people well given this new context in which we find ourselves?" And Andrew Root cautions that when innovation becomes detached from formation and theological depth, it risks responding to cultural anxiety rather than cultivating faithful presence.

Interpretation is what happens when we bring our theological commitments into honest conversation with what we're observing. The five compound drivers are not neutral forces — they carry assumptions about authority, legitimacy, pace, and value. Interpreting them faithfully requires us to examine the lenses we're using and to remain curious about whose interpretation is missing from the room.

This movement invites you to ask not just "what does this driver mean?" but "what does this driver reveal about what we believe — about God, about the church, about human flourishing?"

Reflect
  1. How has your theological tradition shaped what you see as legitimate innovation — and what falls outside that frame? Which voices or forms of experimentation does your tradition tend to trust, and which does it overlook?
  2. The field guide identifies "innovation-washing" — adopting innovation language without deeper learning or structural change. Where might this be happening in your context? What makes it difficult to name?
  3. Where do you feel genuine theological tension with the futures being described — between faithfulness and relevance, tradition and change, conviction and openness? What does that tension reveal?
Go Deeper

What if the discomfort you feel with a particular direction for innovation is not resistance to be overcome, but a theological signal worth attending to?

In Conversation

Gather with two or three people whose theological perspectives differ from your own and try this:

Name one of the five compound drivers. Share how you interpret its significance for innovation in your context. Then invite each person to interpret the same driver differently — not to correct you, but to expand what is visible. What do you hear that you could not have seen alone?

Practice

This week, engage one resource outside your usual interpretive frame — a theological voice from a different tradition, a practitioner working in a different cultural context, or a perspective on innovation from outside the Christian sphere entirely.

Come to it not as a critic but as a learner. Ask: What do they see about the conditions for innovation that I don't? What does their interpretation illuminate about my own assumptions?

Notice

After sitting with this movement, reflect:

What has shifted in how you're making theological meaning of innovation in your context? Are you holding your interpretations more loosely — or has something become clearer? Where has Dean's framing — "how can we love people well?" — landed in you?
