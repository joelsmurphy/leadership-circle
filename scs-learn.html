01 Encounter

Someone has been through it.
Hear what it cost and what it produced.

This is not a success story. It is an account of what the learning actually felt like — and what it required of the leader who lived it.

"I spent years trying to protect the congregation from self-curated spirituality — treating it like a leak I needed to plug. People would come in having deconstructed half of what they'd believed, and my instinct was to help them find their way back to orthodoxy. I was well-intentioned. But I was also, I think, afraid. Afraid that if I sat with their questions without resolving them, I was somehow endorsing the dissolution of faith. It took a long time before I could see that the hunger underneath those questions was real and holy — and that my job was not to resolve the hunger but to be present to it in a way that pointed toward something deeper than what any algorithm was offering them."
Pastor, mid-sized urban congregation, Atlantic Canada
Reflecting on fifteen years of ministry during which the spiritual landscape of her community shifted dramatically — from high-belonging institutional faith to fluid, hybrid, and largely self-curated practice among the people she served

03 Reframe

What has this circle most produced in you?

Three orientations. Select the one that most honestly names what working through these movements has generated. You can only choose one — the constraint is the point.

What Has Shifted
Something in how I see, understand, or respond to self-curated spirituality has genuinely changed. I am not in the same place I was at the beginning of this circle. The shift is real, even if I'm still locating what it means for how I lead.

If this resonates: name the shift as specifically as you can. Vague formation claims are harder to carry than precise ones.

What Remains Uncertain
The most honest output of this circle for me is deeper complexity — not the absence of learning, but a more accurate account of how genuinely difficult faithful engagement of self-curated spirituality actually is. I am holding more questions than I was.

If this resonates: name the central uncertainty. Staying with unresolved questions is itself a formation posture — particularly in a culture where resolution comes cheap.

What Posture Is Forming
More than a specific change or question, I am noticing a different way of standing in relation to self-curated spirituality — a posture forming in me through the practice of attending, interpreting, discerning, and imagining honestly.

If this resonates: name the posture as precisely as you can. Postures are harder to see than insights but more durable — they shape how you respond before you know you're responding.

Challenge

Formation That Changes Nothing

It is possible to work through this circle, feel its weight, articulate its insights carefully — and have nothing actually change about how you lead or how your community engages self-curated spirituality. Not because the work wasn't genuine, but because formation without community accountability tends to remain in the register of reflection rather than practice.

"Who in your actual leadership context knows you have worked through this circle? What would it mean to share your three sentences with them and ask them to name what they see changing in how you lead over the next twelve months? What is the cost of not doing that — to you, and to the people your leadership is meant to serve?"

Micro-Practice — The Witness Conversation

Within the next week, share your three sentences with one other leader in your community. Ask them two things only: Does this match what you see in me? and Will you check in with me in three months about the third one? Formation that lives only in private reflection rarely survives the next leadership crisis. Formation shared and witnessed tends to outlast it.

Why it matters

The pastor in the voice card needed years before the learning became a different leadership posture. The communities of honest accountability around us — the people who see us clearly enough to name what they observe — are one of the conditions that makes that kind of slow formation possible.

Group version

Each person in the leadership group shares their three sentences. After everyone has shared, ask together: What themes appear across all of us? What is the collective formation this circle is producing in our team — and what does that mean for how we lead together going forward?

05 Integrate

Name your Self-Curated Spirituality leadership posture.

Not a conclusion — a beginning. A leadership posture formed by attending to, interpreting, discerning within, imagining beyond, and naming your readiness for ministry in a self-curating spiritual landscape. Carry it forward.

Self-Curated Spirituality Leadership Summary
What I'm attending to

The specific form of self-curated spirituality in your context — named honestly and concretely

The posture I'm forming

The way you are learning to stand in relation to people who are self-curating their faith — not a strategy, but a leadership orientation

The question I'm holding

Tension

The Gift the Church Has Always Had

Self-curated spirituality offers real things: autonomy, authenticity, flexibility, access. It carries genuine spiritual longing and, sometimes, genuine spiritual formation. But it cannot offer what only embodied community provides: the friction and formation of shared life, the accountability of covenant, the depth of a tradition held across generations, and the witness of a community that believes something together and lives accordingly.

The church is not obsolete in a self-curating culture. But it may need to become something different from what it has been — less a vendor of religious services and more a community of serious formation; less a club for the spiritually settled and more a home for the spiritually searching; less a defender of its own forms and more a carrier of the gifts those forms were always meant to hold. That kind of becoming is not a management challenge. It is a formation challenge. And it begins with leaders who are being formed themselves.

For teams and leadership groups — Closing the Circle Together +
  1. Read the voice card aloud. Ask the group: Where are we in the arc she describes — still in the protection mode, or beginning to find a different posture? Name it honestly.
  2. Each person shares their three integration sentences. After each person, invite one word of response from the rest of the group — not evaluation, but witness.
  3. Name the collective formation the circle has produced in your team: What has shifted in how you understand self-curated spirituality and your community's relationship to it? What are you seeing differently together?
  4. Name one concrete commitment your leadership team will make over the next twelve months as a result of working through this circle — not a program, but a practice of leadership formation.
  5. Close with a prayer, a moment of silence, or a shared reading — something that holds the complexity of this moment honestly, and locates your leadership within a tradition that has always been called to engage the spiritual longings of its time without losing what it has been given to carry.
