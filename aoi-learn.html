01 Encounter

Someone has been through it.
Hear what they learned.

This is not a success story. It is an account of what it actually felt like — and what it produced in the leader who lived it.

"I spent the first two years trying to manage the instability — treating it like a problem I could solve if I just worked harder, thought better, found the right framework. The third year, I stopped. Not because I gave up, but because I finally understood that this was the context now, not an interruption to it. That shift changed everything about how I led. I stopped trying to return us to a stability that wasn't coming back and started asking: what does faithfulness look like from here? That question was a lot harder. But it was finally the right one."
Lead Pastor, mid-sized urban congregation
Reflecting on navigating denominational restructuring, significant demographic change, and COVID recovery simultaneously

03 Reframe

What has this circle most produced in you?

Three orientations. Select the one that most honestly names the primary output of working through these movements. You can only choose one — the constraint is the point.

What Has Changed
Something in how I see, interpret, or respond to instability has genuinely shifted. I am not in the same place I was at the start of this circle. The change is real, even if I can't yet fully name it.

If this resonates: name the change as specifically as you can. Vague formation claims are harder to carry than precise ones.

What Remains Uncertain
The most honest output of this circle for me is deeper uncertainty — not the absence of learning, but a more honest account of how much I don't yet know about leading faithfully in instability. I am holding more questions than I was.

If this resonates: name the central uncertainty. Living with unresolved questions is itself a formation posture.

What Posture Is Forming
More than a specific change or a specific question, I am noticing a way of standing in relation to instability that is different from where I started — a posture forming in me through the practice of attending, interpreting, discerning, and imagining.

If this resonates: name the posture as precisely as you can. Postures are harder to see but more durable than insights.

Challenge

Formation That Doesn't Ask Anything

It is possible to work through a formation experience, feel its weight, articulate its insights — and have nothing actually change about how you lead. Not because the insights aren't real, but because formation without accountability to a specific community in a specific season tends to remain in the register of reflection rather than practice.

"Who in your actual leadership community — a colleague, a board member, a spiritual director — knows you have worked through this circle? What would it mean to share your formation summary with them, and ask them to name what they see changing in how you lead over the next twelve months? What is the cost of not doing that?"

Micro-Practice — The Accountability Pair

Within the next week, share your three sentences with one other leader in your community. Ask them two things: Does this match what you see in me? and Will you ask me in three months how the third one is going? Formation that lives only in private reflection rarely survives contact with the next crisis. Formation shared and witnessed tends to.

Why it matters

The leader in the voice card needed three years before the learning became a leadership posture. Accountability to others who can name what they see in you is one of the conditions that makes that kind of learning possible.

Group version

Each person in the leadership group shares their three sentences. After everyone has shared, invite the group to name: what themes do we hear across all of us? What is the collective formation this circle is producing in our team? What does that mean for how we lead together next year?

Tension

Formation That the Age of Instability Requires

The formation this circle attempts is not primarily cognitive — it is not about knowing more about instability. It is about becoming a different kind of leader: one who can hold complexity without collapsing it, sit with uncertainty without being paralyzed, imagine faithfully without losing contact with what is real, and act with courage without pretending to have certainty they don't have.

This is a formation that most leadership development programs are not equipped to produce. It requires the slow, sustained, communal work of attending, interpreting, discerning, imagining, and naming — over and over, in the actual conditions of instability. The circle is not the end of that work. It is a practice of that work. Return to it. Bring others into it. Let it form you over time.

For teams and leadership groups — Closing the Circle Together +
  1. Read the voice card aloud. Ask the group: Where are we in this arc — still in the management phase, or beginning to ask the different question?
  2. Each person shares their three integration sentences. After each person, invite one word of response from the rest of the group — not evaluation, but witness.
  3. Together, name the collective formation the circle has produced: What has shifted in how we understand our community's relationship to instability? What are we seeing differently together?
  4. Name one concrete commitment your leadership team will make over the next twelve months as a result of working through this circle — not a program, but a practice of leadership.
  5. Close with a prayer, a moment of silence, or a shared reading — something that acknowledges that the work of faithful leadership in instability is not yours alone to carry, and that you are not the first generation to be formed by conditions you did not choose.
