01 Encounter

Something has shifted in your context.
You can feel it. What is it?

Before categories or explanations — a scenario. Read it slowly, and notice what it stirs.

Scenario — September 2025

The church's outreach coordinator arrives at the food bank to find that two volunteers — a retired teacher and a middle-aged landscaper — have not returned this fall. She knows why. The landscaper lost a contract when a client's business closed. The retired teacher is now spending three afternoons a week caring for her aging parent, whose home care was cut in the latest provincial budget revision. A third volunteer, a young man who drove thirty minutes each way, has stopped coming since gas hit $2.30 a litre.

The need the food bank is meeting has grown — more clients, younger families, more people she recognizes from the congregation. But the capacity to meet it has quietly shrunk. Nothing dramatic happened. Nobody announced a crisis. The coordinator updates the volunteer spreadsheet. She does not know what to do with what she is noticing.

Instability doesn't usually arrive as an emergency. It accumulates — in spreadsheets, in absences, in the growing gap between what is needed and what is available.

02 Surface

Where are you starting from?

Choose the statement that most honestly names where you are right now in relation to instability. There's no right answer — only an honest one.

I've been living with instability so long I've stopped noticing it. It's just the baseline now. +
That normalization is worth naming directly. When disruption becomes background noise, we lose the capacity to read it well — and we lose the ability to notice when it escalates. This movement invites you to look again at what you've stopped looking at.
I'm aware of instability all around me, but I feel overwhelmed and don't know where to attend first. +
That overwhelm is appropriate to the moment — and it's worth sitting with rather than managing away. This movement isn't asking you to solve the instability or rank it. It's asking you to name what you're actually noticing, in your actual context, one signal at a time.
I can see some of the forces clearly, but I suspect I'm missing others — especially the ones that don't touch me directly. +
That suspicion is worth trusting. The forces shaping instability are distributed unevenly — and leaders whose communities are relatively insulated often have the least accurate read on what others are absorbing. This movement invites you to widen your attention before you narrow to interpretation.
I'm energized by the moment — I think disruption creates opportunity and I want to move toward it. +
The energy is real, and it may be an important signal. But rapid movement from noticing to response can skip the harder work of understanding what is actually being disrupted — and for whom. Let the attention deepen before you name the opportunity.
I'm honestly afraid of what I see when I look directly at where things are heading. +
Fear is information. The question is what to do with it. This movement isn't asking you to resolve the fear or find reasons to be hopeful. It's asking you to look at what you see — with enough steadiness to name it clearly. Fear looked at directly is often less paralyzing than fear avoided.

03 Reframe

Six macro forces are compounding around you.

These aren't separate problems — they are interlocking forces that amplify each other. The Age of Instability report names six. Select the ones you recognize most clearly in your ministry context.

Technological Acceleration + Info Crisis
AI, automation, and information breakdown reshaping trust and communication
Geopolitical Realignment + Political Volatility
Shifting alliances, polarization, and erosion of shared civic frameworks
Polycrisis + Complexity
Multiple simultaneous crises compounding in ways no single framework can contain
Ecological Crisis
Climate disruption, biodiversity loss, and the fraying of ecological systems communities depend on
Economic Instability
Cost of living, housing precarity, and the fragmentation of economic security across generations
Universal Accelerating Change
The pace of change itself as a force — eroding continuity, disrupting formation, and compressing the time for discernment

04 Apply

Three prompts. Write honestly. The goal is not completeness — it is naming what is actually there.

Challenge

What You're Not Looking At

One of the features of compounding instability is that it generates its own blindspots. The forces that most disrupt our communities are often the ones we have the fewest frameworks to see — because they threaten something we have not yet admitted is threatened.

"Name one instability signal in your context that you have been consistently not looking at directly. Not because it doesn't exist — but because looking at it would require you to name something you have been hoping to avoid. What would it cost you to look?"

Micro-Practice — The Instability Log

At the end of each day this week, write one thing you noticed about your ministry context that surprised you, troubled you, or stayed with you. Don't explain it. Don't fix it. Don't connect it to a macro trend. Just name it in one sentence.

Why it matters

The signals of instability that matter most are often local and small — a volunteer who stopped coming, a conversation that ended differently than expected, a question no one could answer. Attention to the local is how leaders build an accurate read on the macro.

Group version

Begin your next team meeting with a round: each person names one thing they've noticed about their context in the last two weeks that they haven't said aloud yet. Listen without responding or solving. Notice what the group is collectively seeing — and what it is collectively not seeing.

Tension

Who Is Absorbing the Instability?

Not all members of a community experience instability the same way. Those with fewer economic buffers, less institutional stability, or less access to community support tend to absorb instability first and most acutely — while those with more resources tend to notice it later and from a greater distance.

Faithful attention to instability means attending not just to the forces themselves but to who is carrying their weight in your community. The person who is absorbing the most may not be in the room when the analysis happens. Their absence is itself a signal worth attending to.

For teams and leadership groups — Mapping Instability Together +
  1. Before the meeting, invite each person to come prepared with one signal of instability they've been noticing in the community they serve — something specific, local, and concrete.
  2. Go around the table. Each person names their signal in one sentence, without explaining or categorizing it. Others listen without responding.
  3. After the round, invite the group to name which macro force — from the six in this movement — each signal seems connected to. Notice where signals cluster.
  4. Ask together: Which of the six forces are we attending to well as a leadership team? Which are we systematically not seeing or not naming?
  5. Close by identifying one community or group in your context whose experience of instability you do not yet understand well enough — and make a concrete plan to listen.
