01 Encounter

The year is 2035.
Some communities found a way through.

These aren't predictions — they are imaginative projections grounded in emerging signals. Read each slowly. Notice what feels possible, what feels naive, and what stirs something in you.

Snapshot A — 2035 / A Church in the Circular Economy

The congregation sold its under-used wing twelve years ago and used the proceeds to join a neighbourhood commons — a shared building housing a cooperative grocery, a tool library, a childcare co-op, and three community organizations. The church holds Sunday worship in the commons hall, and its members sit on the cooperative's board. The pastor describes her calling simply: "We are a theological presence in a commons that has become its own kind of community."

The congregation is smaller than it was in 2020, but it has more actual neighbours than it's ever had. The building is gone. The community is larger.

Snapshot B — 2035 / An Intersectoral Ministry Hub

What used to be a mid-sized evangelical church in a mid-sized city is now something harder to categorize. It houses a mental health clinic three days a week, partners with a local university on neighbourhood research, runs an elder care network that serves members and non-members alike, and maintains a weekly worship service that draws people who wouldn't call themselves Christian but call this place a community. The lead pastor has stopped explaining what the church is. "We know what we're for," she says. "That's enough."

Snapshot C — 2035 / Trauma-Informed Ministry

After years of watching congregation members struggle with anxiety, eco-grief, economic fear, and political despair, the pastoral team trained in trauma-informed care and redesigned the community's entire rhythm — its worship, its small groups, its leadership formation, its pastoral care framework — around the reality that most people arriving on Sunday are carrying a kind of low-grade ongoing grief that the old programming didn't have language for. The change was not strategic. It was pastoral. It started with one question: "What do people actually need from us right now?"

03 Reframe

What is orienting your imagination?

Imagination in instability is not directionless. It is always shaped by what the community believes it is for. Three orientations are at work in communities navigating the age of instability. Select the one that most honestly describes what is driving yours.

Resilience
The primary imagination is organizational: how does the community survive, adapt, and remain functional through disruption? The goal is continuity. The question is: what does the community need to be able to keep going?

Resilience imagination is often necessary — but if it becomes the primary imagination, it can make survival the purpose and obscure the community's deeper calling.

Witness
The primary imagination is prophetic: how does the community embody and announce an alternative to the forces shaping the instability? The goal is counter-narrative. The question is: what does faithfulness look like when everything around it is unstable?

Witness imagination is theologically rich — but if it becomes disconnected from actual community capacity, it can produce beautiful vision statements and exhausted people.

Presence
The primary imagination is incarnational: how does the community show up with people — in suffering, in grief, in complexity — without needing to fix or explain? The goal is accompaniment. The question is: who needs us to be with them in this?

Presence imagination is often what communities discover they are actually called to when they stop asking what programs to run. It requires spiritual depth and can feel insufficient to those who want measurable impact.

04 Apply

Write your community's 2035 snapshot.

Not a plan. Not a goal statement. An imaginative image — specific, human, grounded in your actual context. Use the scaffold below to build it. Each section feeds into the next.

Imaginative Exercise — 2035 It is 2035. Write toward a specific image of your community — not a goal statement, but something you can actually see. Be particular.

01 / The Scene — Describe a specific moment: a gathering, an encounter, a community practice. Where are you? Who is there? What is happening?

02 / Who is There — Who is present in this community that is not present now? Who has found their way here that your current culture or programming doesn't yet reach?

03 / What Was Released — What did your community have to let go of — a program, a building, a way of doing things, an expectation — to become this community?

04 / What Was Held — What stayed essential, non-negotiable, through all the instability and change?

Challenge

The Imagination Instability Threatens

Sustained instability is not just a practical challenge — it is an imaginative one. Communities under sustained pressure tend toward the minimum viable: keeping the doors open, maintaining the budget, retaining the staff. The imagination contracts to what is survivable. This is not a moral failure. But it is a real loss.

"Name one imaginative capacity your community has lost — or is losing — under the pressure of instability. Not a program you've cut, but a way of seeing or dreaming that has become harder to access. When did you last feel genuinely free to imagine what your community could be?"

Micro-Practice — The Imagination Hour

Set aside one hour this month with one other leader — not to plan, strategize, or solve problems, but to imagine. Choose one question: What would our community look like if instability became a formation context rather than just a pressure to manage? Write freely for twenty minutes, then share what you each wrote. Notice what neither of you can yet see alone.

Why it matters

Imagination is not a luxury in a season of instability — it is a survival skill of a different kind. Communities that cannot imagine a different future tend to respond to pressure by simply doing more of what they've always done, faster and with less margin.

Group version

Read one of the 2035 snapshots aloud at your next team gathering. Ask: If this were our community's story — what would be different about us now? What would we need to release? What would need to become more central? Let the responses surface without judgment.

Tension

Sanctified Imagination in Instability

The Christian tradition does not promise that imagination is easy in a season of disruption. The lament psalms do not resolve into vision. The exile prophets speak hope into conditions that make hope seem foolish. Sanctified imagination is not naive optimism — it is the hard work of seeing what God sees when what you can see looks very limited.

The communities in 2035 that find their way through instability will not be the ones with the best strategies. They will be the ones with enough imaginative capacity to ask, repeatedly and faithfully: What is God doing here — and what does faithful participation in that look like from where we actually stand?

For teams and leadership groups — Imagining Together +
  1. Read all three 2035 snapshots aloud as a group. After each one, invite one word from each person: What does this stir?
  2. Each person writes their own 2035 snapshot for your community — five minutes, no editing. Read them aloud without commentary.
  3. After everyone has shared, invite the group to name: What appears in multiple people's snapshots? What only appears in one — and why might that be?
  4. Ask together: What imagination stance are we most drawn toward — Resilience, Witness, or Presence? What does that reveal about what our community currently believes it is for?
  5. Close by naming one imaginative capacity your team wants to cultivate this year — not a program, but a posture: a way of seeing your context that you want to practice developing together.
