01 Encounter

Someone you know is building
their faith entirely on their own terms.

Before categories or explanations — a scenario. Read it slowly. Notice what it stirs in you.

Scenario — A Conversation After Sunday Service

Your church's worship director pulls you aside after the service. Her younger sister — raised in the same church, confirmed, involved in youth group through high school — has been asking her some hard questions lately. The sister is 29. She hasn't attended regularly in about four years.

She's not hostile to faith. She meditates daily, using a mindfulness app. She listens to a podcast by a progressive Christian theologian she found on Instagram. She goes to a sound healing ceremony once a month with friends. At Christmas and Easter she still comes home and sits in the pew. When her nephew was born, she was moved by the baptism and cried. She told her sister: "I believe in something. I'm just not sure the church is the right container for it anymore."

The worship director asks you: "What do I say to her? What do we have to offer someone like her?"

She isn't gone. She isn't hostile. She is building something — just not with you.

02 Surface

Where are you starting from?

Choose the statement that most honestly names where you are in relation to self-curated spirituality right now. There's no right answer — only an honest one.

I'm concerned. This feels like spiritual drift, and I'm not sure how to respond without sounding defensive. +
That concern is worth sitting with rather than resolving too quickly. The discomfort between genuine care for people and uncertainty about what to say to them is itself a signal. This movement invites you to understand before you respond.
I'm curious. I think something real is happening here, and I want to understand it better before I judge it. +
That curiosity is an important starting posture. Self-curated spirituality is not a simple phenomenon — it carries real spiritual longing alongside genuine theological risk. Staying curious long enough to understand it accurately is the first move.
I'm recognizing this in myself. I've been curating my own spiritual life in ways I haven't named out loud. +
That recognition is rare and worth honouring. Leaders who can name their own spiritual self-curation — the podcast they trust more than their denomination's statements, the practice they've adopted from outside the tradition — are better positioned to engage others honestly. Stay with this.
I'm watching people leave. This isn't abstract — it's the people in my actual community who are quietly drifting away. +
The grief of watching people you know disengage from community faith is real and worth naming. But this movement is asking you to understand the shape of what is happening before you respond to the loss. What are they moving toward — not just away from?
I'm uncertain. I don't know what to think about this — whether it's a crisis, an opportunity, or something else entirely. +
That uncertainty is honest and probably accurate. Self-curated spirituality doesn't resolve cleanly into crisis or opportunity — it holds both simultaneously. This circle won't resolve the ambiguity; it will help you inhabit it more faithfully.

03 Reframe

Six forces are shaping the spiritual landscape around you.

These aren't isolated trends — they are interlocking forces driving a fundamental shift in how people form, hold, and practice faith. Select the ones you recognize most clearly in your context.

Post-Modernity + Hyper-Individualism
Personal experience and individual authority displace inherited tradition as the primary source of spiritual truth
Declining Institutional Trust
Mistrust of religious institutions, clergy authority, and denominational structures — accelerated by scandal, perceived irrelevance, and moral failure
Digital + Algorithmic Formation
Algorithms curate spiritual content, podcasts replace preachers, and online communities substitute for local congregation
Deconstruction + Reconstruction
People are not simply leaving faith — they are dismantling inherited belief structures and rebuilding on terms that feel authentic to them
Fluid and Hybrid Belonging
Irregular attendance, multi-community participation, and private practice blur the line between insider and outsider
Participation Culture + Consumer Expectation
People expect to shape their own spiritual experience rather than receive a pre-formed tradition — church becomes one option among many

Challenge

The Person You're Not Thinking Of

Leaders in established faith communities tend to attend most carefully to the people who are most like them — or who have recently left. Self-curated spirituality is also shaping the people who have never been present: people in your neighbourhood who are genuinely spiritual, sincerely seeking, and who have never considered the church as a possible home.

"Name one person or community of people in your neighbourhood or network who is building a genuine spiritual life entirely outside any church connection. Not someone who left — someone who was never there. What do you actually know about how they are forming faith? What would it cost you to understand their experience more accurately?"

Micro-Practice — The Spiritual Audit

This week, pay attention to how spiritual content moves through the digital environments of your daily life. Notice: what podcasts, social media accounts, apps, or online communities are shaping how people around you form faith? What are they offering that your community currently doesn't — or can't? Write down what you notice without evaluating it.

Why it matters

The formation environment has expanded far beyond Sunday morning. Before leaders can engage self-curated spirituality wisely, they need an accurate picture of the spiritual ecosystem people are actually inhabiting — which is usually richer, stranger, and more spiritually earnest than the narrative of simple secularization suggests.

Group version

Ask your team to each come to the next meeting with one example of self-curated spirituality they've encountered in the past month — in a conversation, on social media, in a family relationship, or in a community setting. Share without analysis first. Then ask together: What spiritual longing is underneath each example?

Tension

The Longing Underneath the Curation

Self-curated spirituality is often interpreted as spiritual consumerism — people picking and choosing what feels good rather than submitting to a tradition. That critique is not without merit. But beneath most acts of spiritual self-curation is a genuine longing: for transcendence, for meaning, for community, for a life that connects to something larger than themselves.

The question for faithful Christian leaders is not whether that longing is real — it is. The question is whether the church is perceived as a place where it can actually be met. Attending to self-curated spirituality means attending to both the form and the longing it carries. They are not the same thing, and conflating them produces responses that miss what is actually happening.

For teams and leadership groups — Mapping the Spiritual Landscape Together +
  1. Before the meeting, each person comes prepared with one signal of self-curated spirituality they've noticed in their relationships, neighbourhood, or online environment.
  2. Go around the table. Each person names their signal in one or two sentences. Others listen without evaluating or categorizing. Notice what is named collectively.
  3. After the round, invite the group to map the signals against the six forces: Which forces are most visible in what we're each noticing?
  4. Ask together: Which of these forces is our community attending to well? Which are we systematically not seeing or not naming — and why?
  5. Close by identifying the spiritual longing underneath the most significant signal the group named. Name it as specifically as you can. Ask: Is our community currently positioned to meet that longing?
