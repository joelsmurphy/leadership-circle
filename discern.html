01 Encounter

A decision is being rushed.
Notice what happens inside you.

Read this scenario slowly. Pay attention to where you feel the pull — what you instinctively want to do, and what you want to avoid.

Scenario — Leadership Team Meeting

The meeting was supposed to end twenty minutes ago. The executive director has just said — again — that they need to make a decision today about the new program. There's funding available, but the window is closing. Two board members are nodding. One is quiet. A staff person who works directly with the community they're trying to serve hasn't spoken in forty minutes.

The program is not a bad idea. There is real need. But something about the way this decision is happening — the pace, the pressure, the unasked questions — doesn't feel right to the quiet board member. She can't quite name it. She knows that if she speaks up now, she will slow things down. She also knows that if she doesn't, something important will be missed.

Urgency often does the work that discernment is supposed to do. The question is whether you notice it happening.

03 Reframe

Six questions that good discernment asks.

These are not sequential steps. They are different angles on the same decision. Select the ones that feel most relevant — or most uncomfortable — for your current situation.

01
Who is not in this conversation that should be — and what would they say?
02
What fear is doing the work that hope should be doing here?
03
What would this decision require us to let go of — and are we prepared to lose it?
04
What kind of community is this decision forming us to be?
05
Is this decision reversible? If not — have we actually treated it as irreversible?
06
Where is there evidence of the Spirit's movement in this — and where is there resistance that might be important?

04 Apply

Work the discernment on your actual situation.

Three prompts, structured to move from seeing to weighing to naming. Take time with each. The triad is not a formula — it is a slowing down.

A
What is actually at stake?

Go beneath the surface decision. What is the deeper thing being decided here — about identity, direction, community, or faithfulness?

B
What needs to be honored?

What commitments, relationships, or values must be honored regardless of which direction this decision goes? What would it look like to betray one of them?

C
What would faithful courage require?

Not the easiest path and not the most heroic — but the path that honest faithfulness points toward. What would it actually take to follow it?

Challenge

The Discernment You've Already Done

Discernment is often presented as a future action — something you will engage in when you get to the hard decisions. But you have already been making discernment calls, shaped by the frameworks you've inherited and the pressures you've absorbed.

"What decision in the last year have you made with too little discernment — not because the choice was wrong, but because the process was too thin? What did you skip — and why?"

Micro-Practice — The Slow Question

Before your next significant decision, write down: What is this decision actually about? Who is not in the room? What am I afraid of getting wrong? These three questions, answered honestly in ten minutes, often change what happens next more than any formal process.

Why it matters

Most flawed decisions aren't made by bad people. They're made by good people who were moving too fast to ask the question that was sitting right in front of them.

Group version

Before a team makes a significant decision, spend five minutes answering aloud: "What is this decision asking us to let go of, and are we ready?" Let the hesitation be heard before the vote is called.

Tension

Discernment and the Pressure to Decide

One of the most underacknowledged pressures on ministry leaders is the organizational expectation to be decisive — to model clarity, direction, and confidence. This expectation can make genuine discernment feel like weakness. Leaders who slow down, name uncertainty, and invite broader voices into decisions can feel like they are failing at leadership.

The tradition says otherwise. Faithful discernment has always included the willingness to say "I don't know yet" — not as a deferral of responsibility, but as an act of honesty about what God's direction actually requires. Decisiveness that skips discernment isn't leadership. It's management under pressure.

For teams and leadership groups — Discernment in the Room +
  1. Before a major decision, create a shared pause. Ask: "Have we actually discerned this — or have we managed the urgency?" Name the difference out loud.
  2. Invite one person whose voice has been quiet to speak first. Ask them to name what feels unresolved, not what the answer should be.
  3. Use the three triad questions as a group: What is actually at stake? What needs to be honored? What would faithful courage require?
  4. Name explicitly what you are afraid of getting wrong. Let the fear be heard — not as a veto, but as information.
  5. Close with a shared posture statement: not a decision, but an agreement on how you will make the decision together.
