03 Movement 03 — Futures of Innovation

Discern

Listening for God's invitation amid complexity and constraint

Orientation

The field guide's third interpretive movement asks a different kind of question than the first two: not "what is happening?" or "what does it mean?" but "what kind of discernment does this future require of us?" Six discernment questions are offered — not as problems to solve, but as companions to sit with, revisit, and use as starting points for prayer, conversation, and communal reflection.

Discernment, in this frame, is not a prelude to strategy. It is itself the practice. The field guide acknowledges that under the conditions named by the compound drivers — fatigue, ambivalence, constrained capacity, accelerating technology — discernment becomes harder to sustain. Tension no longer reliably opens into clarity. Ambivalence becomes precarious. And innovation can tip toward what the guide calls "innovation-washing": doing something new as a way to relieve pressure without actually integrating anything.

This movement invites you to slow down long enough to ask: what is God actually inviting here? Not what would be strategic. Not what would look responsive. What is the genuine invitation?

Reflect

The field guide offers six discernment questions. Sit with the ones that feel most alive or most uncomfortable in your context:

  1. Where is innovation actually happening now — and who gets to name it as legitimate? What experiments, adaptations, or new practices are already taking root that haven't been officially recognized?
  2. Where might you be confusing movement, visibility, or experimentation with faithfulness? What would it look like to pause and ask: what is this initiative actually forming in people?
  3. Where might slowness be a form of faithfulness — and where might it be avoidance? How would you tell the difference in your current context?
Go Deeper

What would you decide about innovation in your context if you were not afraid of getting it wrong — or of being seen as not innovative enough?

In Conversation

Gather two or three trusted people — not necessarily those who share your role or perspective — and try this practice:

Spend five minutes in silence together. Then open with this question: "What is God's invitation to us regarding innovation in this season — not what we should do, but what we are being called to become?" Listen without rushing to conclusions. Let the question stay open longer than feels comfortable.

The practice of listening together, without needing to resolve, is itself the work.

Practice

Choose one of the six discernment questions from the field guide that feels most pressing in your context. Before analyzing it or consulting more voices, commit to sitting with it for three days — in prayer, in journaling, in walks, in quiet.

Notice what arises — not just thoughts, but feelings, memories, resistances, and quiet inklings. Then return to it with what you've noticed. Share what you found with someone you trust.

Notice

After sitting with this movement, reflect:

What is becoming clearer about the kind of discernment this moment requires of you and your community? Where are you sensing a genuine invitation — even if it's uncomfortable? What has this practice of slowing down revealed that faster thinking could not?
