03 Movement 03 — Futures of Innovation

Discern

Listening for God's invitation amid complexity and constraint

Orientation

Discernment is not the same as deciding. It is the practice of attending to what is genuinely being asked of you — not what pressure is demanding, not what would look good from the outside, not what you already planned to do. Under the conditions most Christian leaders currently inhabit — chronic overload, competing loyalties, depleted imagination — discernment becomes harder to sustain. This movement slows the engine of urgency long enough to ask: what is actually being invited here, and am I listening to the right things?

Locate Yourself

Choose the statement that most honestly reflects where you are right now.

I feel pressured to make a decision faster than feels right. +
Name that pressure — to yourself and, if possible, to someone you trust. Speed imposed by anxiety rarely produces faithful discernment. What is driving the urgency, and is it coming from the right place?
I've been in discernment mode so long I'm not sure I'm discerning anymore — I may just be deferring. +
That's a sharp and important distinction. Discernment that doesn't move toward anything can become a comfortable avoidance. What would it look like to name, even tentatively, what you sense you are being called toward?
I feel genuine clarity about what I should do, but I'm afraid of what it will cost. +
That combination — clarity and fear — is often a sign that something real is present. What specifically are you afraid of losing if you follow what seems clear?
The people around me are discerning differently than I am, and I don't know what to do with that. +
Communal discernment is not about achieving consensus — it is about listening together well enough that what emerges is larger than any individual's interpretation. Where might the divergence itself be the invitation?
I'm not sure what faithful innovation even looks like in my context, and the uncertainty is wearing on me. +
That weariness is real and worth honoring. Discernment is not an escape from uncertainty — it is a way of inhabiting it that doesn't require you to manufacture certainty you don't have.
"Under conditions of fatigue, ambivalence, and constrained capacity, discernment becomes harder to sustain. Tension no longer reliably opens into clarity. And innovation can tip toward a way of relieving pressure without actually integrating anything."
Interpretive Field Guide — Futuring Hub
Reflect

The field guide offers six discernment questions. Sit with the ones that feel most alive or most uncomfortable in your context:

  1. Where is innovation actually happening now — and who gets to name it as legitimate? What experiments, adaptations, or new practices are already taking root that haven't been officially recognized?
  2. Where might you be confusing movement, visibility, or experimentation with faithfulness? What would it look like to pause and ask: what is this initiative actually forming in people?
  3. Where might slowness be a form of faithfulness — and where might it be avoidance? How would you tell the difference in your current context?
Challenge

Urgency Is Not a Calling

Urgency has a way of masquerading as necessity. Discernment asks whether what feels urgent is actually what is most important.

"What is the most urgent decision facing your community right now? Now ask: who is creating the urgency? Is the timeline coming from the situation itself — or from anxiety, external pressure, or someone's discomfort with uncertainty?"

Group Practice

Slow Discernment Circle

  1. Gather three to five people. Begin with two to three minutes of silence together.
  2. Name one question your community is currently facing — framed as a genuine question, not a decision to be ratified.
  3. Each person responds, one at a time, with no cross-talk: "What I sense is being asked of us here is..." followed by "What I am afraid of getting wrong is..."
  4. After all have spoken, hold another minute of silence.
  5. Close not with a decision, but with this question: "What is the most important thing we heard that we don't want to lose before we meet again?"
Micro-Practice

The Three-Day Pause

Identify one decision or direction you are currently being pressed toward. Commit to not acting on it for three days. During those three days, carry the question in ordinary life — in walks, in quiet, in prayer, in conversation. Write down what surfaces — not your analysis, but what you notice.

Why it matters

Discernment requires time that most ministry environments refuse to give it. Three days is small enough to be real and large enough to reveal things that urgency hides.

Group version

When a significant decision is on the table, the group agrees not to decide in the same meeting in which the question is first introduced. Allow at least one week for individual reflection before returning to a collective conversation.

Questions

Three Questions Worth Sitting With

  1. Where in your current leadership are you responding to pressure rather than listening to invitation?
  2. What is the difference, in your context, between faithful slowness and institutional avoidance — and how would you tell them apart?
  3. If you removed the fear of being seen as irrelevant or behind, what would you actually discern is being asked of you right now?
Notice

What is becoming clearer about the kind of discernment this moment requires of you and your community? Where are you sensing a genuine invitation — even if it's uncomfortable? What has this practice of slowing down revealed that faster thinking could not?
