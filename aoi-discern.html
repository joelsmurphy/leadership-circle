01 Encounter

The decision was made in fear.
Everyone knew it. No one said it.

Read this slowly. Notice what instinct it activates — and what it asks of you.

Scenario — Board Meeting, Early 2026

The denomination had just announced restructuring — fewer staff, merged regional offices, a new emphasis on congregational self-sufficiency. The church's board was meeting to decide whether to cut the part-time associate pastor position. The lead pastor sat quietly as three board members made the financial case. The numbers were real. The anxiety was realer.

What nobody said out loud: the associate pastor had been the primary person doing ministry with the younger families who had recently started attending. Her work was the only thread connecting the congregation to the neighbourhood's most recent arrivals — people who were themselves navigating economic precarity, language barriers, and housing instability.

The board voted to eliminate the position. It was a reasonable financial decision made under genuine pressure. Two months later, the young families stopped coming. The board had discerned the budget. They had not discerned the community.

In seasons of instability, reactive management can do the work that discernment is supposed to do. The difference is often invisible in the moment — and only clear in the aftermath.

03 Reframe

Six questions that discernment in instability asks.

These aren't sequential. They are different angles on a decision being made in an unstable context. Select the ones that feel most necessary — or most uncomfortable — for your current situation.

01
Who is being squeezed out of this decision by the instability — and what would they say if we found a way to include them?
02
What fear is doing the work that discernment should be doing here — and are we willing to name it out loud?
03
What community relationship or ministry capacity might this decision quietly eliminate — and have we honestly named what that loss would mean?
04
What kind of community is this decision forming us to be — not in intention, but in effect?
05
What would prophetic faithfulness require here — not the safest path, but the one that takes the gospel seriously in this specific moment?
06
Where is there evidence of the Spirit's movement in this instability — and where is there a form of resistance that might itself be guidance?

04 Apply

Work the discernment on your actual situation.

Three prompts, structured to move from the surface pressure to the deeper stakes to the faithful path. This is not a formula — it is a slowing down inside the instability.

A
What is actually threatened?

Beneath the budget line, the staffing decision, the program cut — what is the deeper thing being threatened? Community relationships? A ministry to the margin? A way of being church? Name it precisely.

B
What must be honored?

What relationships, commitments, or theological convictions must be honored even in this season of pressure — regardless of which direction the decision goes? What would it mean to betray one of them?

C
What would prophetic faithfulness require?

Not the safest path. Not the most heroic one. The one that honest faithfulness actually points toward in this specific moment of instability — even if it costs something significant. What would it take to follow it?

Challenge

The Discernment Instability Steals

One of the most underacknowledged costs of instability is what it does to the conditions for discernment. When communities are under sustained pressure, discernment gets compressed into management. Leaders who slow down in a crisis can feel like they are failing. The expectation of decisive action is often inversely proportional to the time available for genuinely faithful decision-making.

"Name a decision your community has made in the last two years where instability — financial pressure, staff loss, demographic change — compressed the discernment process. What was skipped? What would it have looked like to have discerned it more faithfully? What stopped that from happening?"

Micro-Practice — The Instability Pause

Before your next significant decision under pressure, take ten minutes to write three things: What is the actual fear driving the urgency? Who is not in this conversation? What would we do if we weren't afraid? These three questions, answered honestly, often change what happens next more than any strategic process.

Why it matters

The board in the scenario didn't lack good intentions. They lacked the conditions for discernment — and nobody stopped to name that the conditions were wrong. Creating a deliberate pause is a leadership act, not a weakness.

Group version

Before a team makes a significant decision in a season of pressure, name out loud: "We are making this decision under instability conditions. What are we at risk of skipping?" Let the naming of the risk slow the room down enough to actually check.

Tension

Discernment and Prophetic Faithfulness

The tradition of prophetic leadership in Scripture is not a tradition of decisiveness under pressure. It is a tradition of uncomfortable fidelity — of leaders who said things that couldn't be managed, who named what others were avoiding, who oriented the community not toward safety but toward faithfulness. Prophetic leadership in instability is not loud or dramatic. It is the quiet insistence on asking the question that fear is trying to prevent from being asked.

The question is not whether your community has the capacity for prophetic discernment. It is whether the conditions for that kind of discernment are being protected — or being quietly eliminated by the same instability you're trying to navigate.

For teams and leadership groups — Discernment Under Pressure +
  1. Before a significant decision, name explicitly: "We are making this under instability conditions. What are the conditions for discernment that we might be tempted to skip?" Let the group respond honestly.
  2. Invite one person to play the role of community advocate: their job is to speak for those who are not in the room — especially those most affected by the instability — and name what they might say about this decision.
  3. Use the triad as a group: What is actually threatened? What must be honored? What would prophetic faithfulness require?
  4. Name the fear that is most active in the room. Give it a name. Ask: Is this fear giving us accurate information, or is it narrowing our field of vision?
  5. Close with a shared discernment posture statement — not a decision, but a commitment to how the decision will be made: what voices will be included, what questions will be held open, what will not be sacrificed for the sake of urgency.
