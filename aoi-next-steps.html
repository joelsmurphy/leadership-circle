Next Steps

Five practice pathways for faithful leadership in the age of instability

These pathways are drawn directly from the leadership implications in the Futuring Hub's Age of Instability report. They are not programs to implement or frameworks to adopt. They are invitations — starting points for communities that have done the sensemaking work of this circle and are ready to ask what comes next.

Each pathway names a practice, the theological conviction underneath it, and a first concrete step. Begin with whichever most honestly connects to where your community is right now.

01 Pathway

Normalize Disruption as a Discipleship Context

Stop treating instability as an interruption to normal ministry. Start treating it as the formation context in which disciples are actually being made right now.

The Practice

Redesign at least one regular gathering — a small group, a sermon series, a leadership meeting — to explicitly name the instabilities your community is living with and ask: What does following Jesus look like from here? Not as a crisis response, but as a regular formation practice.

The goal is to create conditions in which members can bring the actual instability of their lives into the community's formational space — not just their spiritual questions in abstraction, but the economic pressure, the ecological grief, the political exhaustion, the relational disruption that is the actual texture of their discipleship right now.

Theological Conviction

Discipleship Was Never Designed for Stable Conditions

The New Testament was written to communities under pressure — communities navigating occupation, persecution, poverty, and radical social upheaval. The formation practices at the heart of the tradition — lament, prayer, communal discernment, sharing of resources, mutual care — were designed precisely for conditions like these. The church does not need to adapt its formation practices to instability. It needs to recover the ones it already has.

First Step

At your next leadership team gathering, set aside fifteen minutes and ask: "In what spaces of our community's life right now are people able to bring the real instability of their lives — not just their spiritual questions, but the actual disruption they're carrying? Where is that absent?" Name what you find honestly.

02 Pathway

Cultivate Adaptive and Relational Leadership

Invest deliberately in the kind of leadership that instability actually requires — not more competency, but more adaptive capacity, relational depth, and emotional resilience.

The Practice

Identify two or three leaders in your community — formal or informal — who are navigating instability with unusual grace, honesty, or adaptive courage. Create a structured space to learn from them: not a workshop, but a sustained conversation over six to twelve months. Ask them to name what they have learned and what it cost.

In parallel, build regular emotional and spiritual sustainability practices into leadership culture — not as perks, but as structural commitments. Regular supervision or spiritual direction for lead staff, sabbath practices taken seriously at the leadership level, and accountability structures that make it safe to name depletion before it becomes crisis.

Theological Conviction

Leadership Formation Is Never Only Individual

The leaders who navigate instability most faithfully over time are not the ones with the best personal resilience strategies. They are the ones embedded in communities of honest accountability — people who see them clearly, name what they see, and refuse to let leadership become isolated performance. Adaptive leadership is formed in relationship, not in solitude.

First Step

Name one leader in your community who you believe is navigating instability with unusual faithfulness. Arrange a conversation with them — not to extract their lessons, but to listen to their experience. Ask them: What has instability taught you that you couldn't have learned any other way? What has it cost you? What has held you?

03 Pathway

Reframe the Church as Compass, Not Fortress

Move deliberately away from stability-as-purpose and toward presence-as-purpose — becoming a community that helps people navigate instability rather than one that provides shelter from it.

The Practice

Audit your community's current programming, communication, and culture through one question: Is this designed to stabilize insiders, or to orient people — members and neighbours alike — through disruption? The audit is not meant to produce wholesale change. It is meant to make visible what is currently invisible: the degree to which the community's default orientation is inward and protective rather than outward and navigational.

Then identify one concrete shift — one space, one practice, one posture — that moves the community toward a compass orientation without abandoning the genuine stability it has to offer.

Theological Conviction

The Gospel Has Always Been Navigational

The earliest name for Christian community was "the Way" — not a destination, but a direction; not a shelter, but a path. The communities that most faithfully reflect the gospel in an age of instability will be the ones that help people orient their lives in the direction of God's kingdom — not the ones that most successfully insulate their members from the disruption everyone around them is experiencing.

First Step

Ask three people who are marginally connected to your community — people who have attended occasionally but not regularly — what they experience when they come. What do they feel they are being offered? What do they feel is missing? Listen without defending. What the margins see is often the most accurate read on what the centre is actually communicating.

04 Pathway

Engage in Prophetic Imagination and Counter-Narrative

Become a community that practices sanctified imagination in public — one that names the dominant narratives about instability and offers an alternative grounded in the gospel's vision of human flourishing.

The Practice

Identify the dominant narratives about instability circulating in your community's context — the stories being told about what is happening, who is responsible, what the future holds, and what faithful response looks like. Name them specifically. Then begin the slow work of articulating the counter-narrative your community holds: what does the gospel say about this instability? What does it promise? What does it require?

This is not a communications strategy — it is a formation practice. The goal is a community that can speak coherently about what it believes in the actual conditions of the instability people around it are living through. Prophetic imagination is the capacity to name what God sees when what is visible looks very discouraging.

Theological Conviction

Prophetic Faithfulness Is Not Optimism

The biblical prophets did not offer optimism. They offered a different account of reality — one that named what was happening honestly and located it within a larger story of God's faithfulness. Prophetic imagination in the age of instability is not cheerful resilience. It is the hard, slow work of holding the church's understanding of history, humanity, and God's purposes up against the specific conditions of disruption and asking: What is true here that cannot be seen without faith?

First Step

Name the single dominant narrative about instability that your community is most susceptible to absorbing — the story that, if you're not careful, will replace the gospel narrative. Write it out precisely. Then write the counter-narrative your community holds. How different are they? Where does your community's theological convictions actually challenge the dominant account — and where are they being quietly shaped by it?

05 Pathway

Build Emotional and Spiritual Resilience

Invest in the deep formation that sustains faithful leadership and community life through prolonged instability — not resilience as endurance, but resilience as the capacity to remain present, honest, and oriented.

The Practice

Build lament into the community's regular life. Not as a special service for difficult seasons, but as a structural feature of how the community processes what it is living through — in worship, in small groups, in leadership meetings. Lament is not despair. It is the refusal to pretend things are better than they are — and the act of bringing that honest acknowledgment into the presence of God rather than managing it away.

In parallel, cultivate practices of contemplative attention — sabbath, silence, spiritual direction, retreat — that create the conditions for the kind of deep, slow discernment that instability tends to erode. These are not luxury practices. They are the conditions under which communities can remain spiritually oriented rather than merely reactive.

Theological Conviction

Resilience Is Formed, Not Found

The spiritual resilience that sustains communities through instability is not a natural capacity that some communities happen to have. It is a formed capacity — shaped by regular practices of lament, contemplation, communal discernment, and shared theological reflection that build the community's capacity to hold difficulty without collapsing into either denial or despair. Communities that want to navigate instability faithfully need to invest in formation practices before the instability intensifies, not after.

First Step

Identify one formation practice your community currently treats as optional — sabbath, silent retreat, lament prayer, regular spiritual direction for leadership — and have a conversation about whether it could become structural. Not a new program. A shift in what the community treats as necessary for faithful life together. What would it take to move it from the edge to the centre?
