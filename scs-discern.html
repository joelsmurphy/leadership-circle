01 Encounter

The response was defensible.
But something in it was wrong.

Read this slowly. Notice what it stirs — where you feel the pull in different directions.

Scenario — A Denominational Forum, 2024

The denomination had been working for two years on a "spiritual formation framework" — a document intended to guide churches in responding to spiritual self-curation. The lead writer presented the draft to a regional gathering. It was careful, theologically grounded, and defensively clear. It articulated what authentic Christian formation looked like. It named what was insufficient about self-curated approaches. It was hard to disagree with any of it.

At the end of the presentation, a young pastor raised her hand. She said: "I understand what this is trying to do. But I have people in my congregation right now who are deconstructing their inherited faith as carefully and prayerfully as I've ever seen anyone engage theology. And I have people who have been 'properly formed' by our tradition and whose faith is hollow and performative. I'm not sure this document is talking to the people I actually know."

There was a long silence. The framework was theologically correct. But it was operating in a world that was neater than the one the young pastor was actually inhabiting.

Discernment asks not only what is true, but what faithful truth-telling looks like in the actual conditions where people are trying to follow.

02 Surface

What is actually driving your community's response to self-curated spirituality?

Three layers. Good discernment about self-curated spirituality requires naming what is actually operating underneath the stated response — which is often more reactive than reflective.

Layer A

What Feels Most at Stake

Think of a specific situation in your context — a person who has left, a conversation you've had, a decision about formation or belonging your community has made or is considering. What feels most at stake in how you respond?

Layer B

What Is Driving the Response

Most institutional responses to self-curated spirituality are shaped more by anxiety than by discernment. Name honestly what is driving your community's current posture — not what it should be driven by, but what it actually is.

Layer C

What Faithful Response Is Skipping

The young pastor in the scenario identified a gap between the framework and the actual people she serves. What is your community's current response to self-curated spirituality skipping — what question isn't being asked, what voice isn't being heard, what condition isn't being honestly faced?

03 Reframe

Six questions that discernment asks in a self-curating spiritual culture.

These aren't sequential steps. They are different angles on the same challenge. Select the ones that feel most necessary — or most uncomfortable — for your current situation.

01
What understanding of spiritual authority is our community embodying — and is it one we would actually choose if we thought about it carefully?
02
What does genuine formation look like in a fluid spiritual environment — and is what we're offering actually producing it?
03
What kind of belonging are we creating — and are the conditions for that belonging accessible to people who are mid-deconstruction or spiritually searching?
04
What fear is shaping our response to self-curated spirituality — and is that fear giving us accurate information, or is it narrowing our vision?
05
What does the tradition actually have to offer someone who is building their own spiritual life — that they might not be able to find by curating alone?
06
What would prophetically faithful witness look like in a self-curating culture — neither retreating into insularity nor dissolving into ambient spirituality?

04 Apply

Work the discernment on your actual situation.

Three prompts, structured to move from the real conditions to what must be honored to what faithful courage actually requires. This is not a formula — it is a slowing down inside complexity.

A
What is actually being asked of your community?

Beneath the surface challenge of declining attendance, shifting authority, or deconstructing members — what is actually being asked? What deeper question about belonging, formation, or faithful presence is the self-curating landscape putting to your community?

B
What must be honored?

What theological convictions, relational commitments, or communal values must be honored as your community navigates this — regardless of what form the response takes? What would it mean to betray one of them — either by retreating into defensive boundary-maintenance or by accommodating in ways that dissolve what the gospel distinctively offers?

C
What would faithful courage require?

Not the safest path. Not the most heroic one. The path that honest faithfulness points toward in your specific context — even if it costs something. This might mean changing something your community has been unwilling to change, or holding something your community has been quietly letting go.

Challenge

The Discernment That Defends Rather Than Seeks

Much of what passes for discernment in response to self-curated spirituality is actually defence — careful theological articulation of why the church's existing approach is correct and the alternatives are insufficient. Genuine discernment is different: it asks not only what is true, but what God is doing in this moment — including in the longing and searching that self-curated spirituality expresses.

"Name one aspect of self-curated spirituality in your context that, if you took it seriously as a spiritual signal rather than a problem to be solved, would require you to change something about how your community forms, belongs, or leads. What has been preventing you from taking it that seriously?"

Micro-Practice — The Three Questions

Before your next significant formation or programming decision, pause and write three things: What spiritual longing is this decision serving? Who does this make it easier to belong — and who does it make it harder? What would we do if we weren't afraid? The answers to these three questions, taken seriously, often change what happens next more than any strategy.

Why it matters

Formation decisions made primarily from anxiety about self-curated spirituality tend to produce communities that are more defensive and less hospitable than before — which accelerates the very departure they were trying to prevent.

Group version

Before a team makes a significant decision about formation, belonging, or pastoral response to self-curated spirituality, name out loud: "What are we afraid of in this situation — and is our fear giving us accurate information?" Let the naming of the fear slow the room down enough to actually check.

Tension

Faithfulness Is Not the Same as Correctness

The denomination's framework in the encounter scenario was theologically correct. That is not a small thing. Theological clarity matters. But faithfulness in a self-curating culture requires something beyond correctness — it requires proximity. It requires leaders who know the actual people who are searching, deconstructing, curating, and longing, well enough to speak into their specific conditions rather than into a generalized cultural pattern.

The tradition's resources for this moment are real. The question is whether those resources can be carried by communities that are close enough to the actual people who need them. Correctness without proximity produces frameworks. Proximity with theological depth produces pastoral discernment. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient without the other.

For teams and leadership groups — Discernment in Community +
  1. Read the encounter scenario together. Ask: Has our community ever produced something like that framework — theologically correct but not quite speaking to the actual people we know? Name an example honestly.
  2. Use the triad questions as a group: What is actually being asked of us? What must we honor? What would faithful courage require? Give each prompt its own ten minutes.
  3. Invite one person to speak for the people who are self-curating their spirituality outside your community — to name what those people might want your community to hear that it hasn't yet been willing to listen to.
  4. Name together: Where is our community's response to self-curated spirituality most driven by anxiety — and where is it most driven by genuine pastoral conviction? Are those the same thing?
  5. Close with a shared discernment posture statement — not a policy, but a commitment to how your community will hold this question together over the next season.
